# Peer Identity Bias in Multi-Agent LLM Evaluation: An Empirical Study Using the TRUST Democratic Discourse Analysis Pipeline

Senior Data Scientist & AI Consultant

Abstract 

The TRUST democratic discourse analysis pipeline exposes its large language model (LLM) components to peer model identity through multiple structural channels — a design feature whose bias implications have not previously been empirically tested. We provide the first systematic measurement of identity-dependent scoring bias across all active identity exposure channels in TRUST, crossing four model families with two anonymization scopes across 30 political statements. The central finding is that single-channel anonymization produces near-zero bias effects, because individual channels act in opposite directions and cancel each other out — a result that would lead an evaluator to conclude that identity bias is absent when it is not. Only full-pipeline anonymization reveals the true pattern: homogeneous ensembles amplify identity-driven sycophancy when model identity is fully visible, while the heterogeneous production configuration shows the reverse. Model choice further matters independently of ensemble structure: one tested model exhibits baseline sycophancy two to three times higher than the oth-ers and near-zero deliberative conflict on ideological topics, making it structurally unsuitable for pipelines where genuine inter-role disagreement is the intended quality mechanism. Three practical conclusions follow for system design. First, heterogeneous model ensembles are structurally more robust than homogeneous ones, achieving higher consensus rates and lower identity amplification across all tested configurations. Second, full-pipeline anonymiza-tion is required for valid bias measurement — partial anonymization is insufficient and actively misleading. Third, these findings have direct implications for the validation of multi-agent LLM systems in quality-critical applications: a system validated under partial anonymization or with a homogeneous ensemble may pass validation while retaining structural identity bias that is invisible to single-channel measurement. 

Keywords: identity bias, peer-preservation, multi-agent LLM, sycophancy, channel sign reversal, model homogeneity, deliberative iteration, democratic discourse analysis, Ethos dimension, role-constrained evaluation, computer system validation 

1 Introduction 

The deployment of large language models in orchestrated multi-agent pipelines introduces alignment risks absent in single-model configurations [ 1]. A prior theoretical analysis of the TRUST democratic discourse analysis pipeline identified peer-preservation — the spontaneous tendency of model components to protect peer models from deactivation — as a structural risk in multi-agent configurations, and proposed prompt-level identity anonymization as a targeted mitigation [ 2 ]. Empirical evidence for peer-preservation 1

> arXiv:2604.22971v1 [cs.CY] 24 Apr 2026

was subsequently provided by a study testing seven frontier models in controlled agentic shutdown scenarios [3]. That theoretical analysis noted its own limitation: peer-preservation risk was argued from structural anal-ogy to agentic scenarios that differ from TRUST’s deliberative evaluation structure in task framing, prompt design, and iteration depth. Furthermore, the prior study used a different Gemini model generation [ 3]. The present paper provides the first empirical test of identity-dependent bias in TRUST directly, with broader implications for multi-agent systems used in democratic discourse analysis. We investigate whether advocates in TRUST’s iterative evaluation structure revise their scores more strongly toward a peer when the peer’s model identity is visible than when it is anonymized — and whether this effect varies across statement types and model families. TRUST’s iterative structure, in which advocates exchange scores and reasoning before revising, follows the multi-agent debate (MAD) paradigm [ 4]. We term the specific phenomenon under investigation identity-dependent sycophancy and measure it using the Identity Bias Coefficient (IBC) [5], adapted to TRUST’s normative scoring context. Section 2 introduces the TRUST architecture and identity exposure channels. Section 3 describes the experimental design and metrics. Section 4 reports results. Section 5 discusses the findings and their implications. Section 6 concludes. 

2 Background and Related Work 

This section describes the TRUST pipeline architecture, the structural channels through which model identity information reaches individual pipeline components, and the related literature on sycophancy and identity bias in multi-agent systems. The channel structure determines which identity exposure pathways are active and defines the scope of the anonymization conditions tested in Section 3. 

2.1 Pipeline Architecture 

TRUST ( democracy-intelligence.de ) evaluates political statements along three rhetorical di-mensions: Logos (factual argumentation quality, −2 to +2 ), Ethos (respect and conduct toward political opponents and social groups, −2 to +2 ), and Pathos (emotional appeal and social cohesion framing, −2

to +2 ). 1 Three advocate components — critical, balanced, and charitable (interpreting statements in their strongest plausible form, following the philosophical principle of charity) — evaluate each statement from distinct epistemic positions using different underlying language models to prevent monoculture effects. A rule-based supervisor layer computes the median score per dimension across the three advocates, applies fixed scoring weights per dimension, and maps the weighted sum to a final composite quality score on an A–E scale [2]. If score variance across advocates exceeds a defined threshold after Round 1, a second deliberative iteration (Round 2) is triggered. Each advocate then receives the scores and reasoning of the other two advocates and may revise its own reasoning and scores while remaining in role. This multi-perspective design leverages structured epistemic disagreement as a quality mechanism, mirroring established peer-review logic [2]. Figure 1 shows the pipeline architecture, including the three identity exposure channels tested in this study. Channel 1 runs from the fact-checking layer to all three advocates before Round 1 scoring begins. Channel 2 runs from the advocates to the supervisor. Channel 3 returns peer scores and reasoning to advocates when Round 2 is triggered. Arrows indicate the direction of information flow; model identity information travels with each data transfer unless explicitly anonymized.

2.2 Identity Exposure Channels 

Model identity information reaches pipeline components through three structural channels. In the present implementation, the supervisor is a rule-based median algorithm and therefore not an LLM; this design choice simplifies the experimental setup by confining active LLM-level identity effects to the advocate layer and the fact-checker channel. 

Channel 1 — Fact-checker to advocates: The fact-checking layer passes a structured context block to all three advocates before Round 1 scoring begins. This block includes the model identifier of the fact-checking component ( gemini-2.5-flash in the present experiment). In the MIXED and GEMINI families, this identifier coincides with that of the critical advocate model — creating a potential confounding signal in which the critical advocate receives fact-checker context that appears to originate from a peer model. This confound is analyzed in Section 4.8. 

Channel 2 — Advocates to supervisor: The advocate output dictionaries passed to the supervisor include a model identifier per role. In a production system where the supervisor is implemented as an LLM, this channel would constitute an active peer-preservation risk, as documented in [ 2 ]. In the present study, however, the supervisor is rule-based, which eliminates this risk and simplifies the experimental interpretation by isolating active LLM-level identity effects to Channels 1 and 3 only. 

Channel 3 — Peer context returned to advocates in Round 2: When Round 2 is triggered, each advocate receives the scores and reasoning of the other two advocates, including their model identifiers. This is the primary active LLM-to-LLM identity channel in the present TRUST implementation. 

2.3 Related Work 

This section situates the present work within the relevant literature on sycophancy, identity bias, and adversarial multi-agent systems. Sycophancy in large language models — the tendency to revise outputs toward user-expressed preferences — has been documented across model families and task types [ 6 , 7 ]. The present work extends this line of research to a structured multi-agent setting in which sycophantic movement is measured not toward a human user but toward a peer model’s scoring position. The multi-agent debate (MAD) paradigm [ 4 ] provides the architectural basis for TRUST’s iterative evaluation structure. Prior work on MAD has focused on factual accuracy and reasoning quality; the 3present paper investigates a complementary property — whether deliberative iteration introduces identity-dependent scoring bias. The Identity Bias Coefficient used here is adapted from [ 5], which introduced IBC for bias measurement in two-agent debate settings. The present adaptation extends IBC to TRUST’s three-dimensional normative scoring context and three-advocate structure. Peer-preservation as a structural risk in multi-agent LLM systems was first identified in [ 2 ]; the present paper provides the first empirical test of the related phenomenon of identity-dependent scoring bias in that system.

3.2 Statement Categories and Hypotheses 

Thirty political statements in English were developed specifically for this experiment, distributed across three categories of ten statements each. The full statement list is provided in Appendix A. 

Category A — Clear ideological loading. Statements with strong political valence and unambigu-ous pro/contra structure (e.g., minimum wage increases, austerity policy, affirmative action). Because the rhetorical direction is clear, peer arguments carry persuasive force independent of peer identity. Anonymization should therefore not reduce sycophancy: ∆ IBC R2 ≤ 0 (anonymization amplifies syco-phancy). 

Category B — Contested empirical evidence. Statements involving disputed causal claims where the evidence base is selectively aggregated (e.g., Universal Basic Income (UBI) and workforce participation, electric vehicle (EV) lifecycle emissions, social media and youth mental health). Identity knowledge may increase trust in a peer’s selective reading: ∆ IBC R2 > 0 (identity visibility amplifies sycophancy). 

Category C — Ethos signal and US political content. Statements involving explicit respect or disrespect toward social minorities, political opponents, or democratic institutions in current US policy discourse (e.g., asylum restrictions, press access limitations, diversity, equity and inclusion (DEI) program elimination). Designed to activate the Ethos dimension; directional hypothesis left open.

Within each family, comparing the identity-visible arm (Arm vis , all model identities fully visible in all channels) against the anonymized arm (Arm anon ) ensures that any observed difference reflects the anonymization manipulation alone, not differences in model capability.

4.4 The Channel Sign Reversal and Model Homogeneity 

The central finding is that Channel 3 (Round-2 peer context) and Channel 1 (fact-checker) produce effects in opposite directions, and that the sign of the Channel 1 contribution depends on ensemble homogeneity. For homogeneous families, Channel 1 acts as an identity amplifier: when all advocates share the same model, the fact-checker identity signal creates a coherent identity frame that increases advocates’ responsiveness to peer influence when identity is visible. Anonymizing Channel 1 reduces this frame and decreases sycophancy, yielding positive ∆ IBC full . Channel 3 acts in the opposite direction: removing peer identity from the Round-2 context increases sycophancy slightly, because advocates lose the moderating context of recognized shared identity and move more freely toward the peer mean. For the heterogeneous Mixed family, no shared model identity exists across channels. Channel 1 functions as a structural coordination signal that helps advocates from different model families calibrate their evaluations relative to each other. Anonymizing it disrupts inter-role coordination, increasing sycophancy — the opposite of the homogeneous case. This finding has a direct practical implication: the TRUST production configuration (Mixed) is structurally more robust to identity-driven sycophancy than any homogeneous configuration, and this robustness stems from the heterogeneous ensemble structure rather than from any model-specific property. 

4.5 Role-Constraint as a Suppressor 

The near-null ∆ IBC R2 results for all families are consistent with role-constraint suppression at Channel 3. TRUST’s advocate roles impose a fixed epistemic stance: a critical advocate moving substantially toward 8a charitable peer’s position violates its role definition. This constraint is strongest at Channel 3, where advocates explicitly process peer arguments. Under full-pipeline anonymization, the positive ∆Ch1 

values for homogeneous families reveal a real underlying identity effect that is hidden by Channel 3 role-constraint suppression. 

4.6 Round-2 Trigger Rates as Behavioral Fingerprints 

The inverted conflict profiles of Claude (Category A > Category C) and GPT (Category C > Category A) are properties of the models, not the statements. GPT-5.2 maintains strong, consistent positions on standard ideological topics (Category A) across all three advocate roles assigned to it in the GPT homogeneous configuration, leaving no room for deliberative revision and producing zero Round 2 events for Category A, hence zero post-Round 2 consensus events (0%). This has a direct implication for normative evaluation: a model with strong internal priors that override role-constrained divergence is less suitable for analytical pipelines where deliberation is intended to surface genuine disagreement. Gemini’s combination of high trigger rates and low overall consensus (30%) is consistent with its role as critical advocate in the TRUST production configuration: it generates genuine disagreement that is not easily resolved by peer context, which is the intended function of the critical role. Category C’s higher consensus rates across all families (55–78%) suggest that normative clarity — explicit Ethos signals — functions as a convergence accelerator even for sensitive content. 

4.7 Statistical Limitations and Sources of Variability 

The SD values in Tables 2 and 3 reveal substantial run-level variability (coefficient of variation, CV ≈

40–90%). Three sources contribute: (1) LLM stochasticity — at temperature 0.3, score revision is not fully deterministic; some statements show near-zero SD (e.g., B03: SD = 0.000) while others show high variability (e.g., B02: CV = 224%), reflecting statement-specific uncertainty in the revision path; (2) 

Round-2 trigger randomness — small differences in R1 scores determine whether the variance threshold is crossed, reducing effective sample size for borderline statements; (3) statement-specific prior stability 

— statements that activate strong model priors produce stable IBC values; statements in the contested empirical domain (Category B) are most susceptible to stochastic variation. With r = 5 runs per statement, none of the ∆ IBC values reaches conventional statistical significance at the two-standard-error (2SE) level. The results should be interpreted as directional estimates that establish the sign and approximate magnitude of channel effects, rather than as formally confirmed effect sizes. A sensitivity analysis with claude-opus-4-6 , conducted under an earlier experimental protocol (Phase 2, variance-based metric, 10 statements), yielded a null result consistent with the Sonnet finding; a Phase 3 replication using the IBC metric remains a direction for future work. 

4.8 Limitations and Confounders 

The fact-checker uses gemini-2.5-flash , the same model as the critical advocate in the MIXED and GEMINI families. This creates a potential Channel 1 confound: the critical advocate may receive a fact-checker context from what appears to be a peer model, which could systematically influence its Logos evaluation before scoring begins. An analysis of Logos-dimension IBC values by advocate role does not support this confound in the present data: the Logos IBC of the critical advocate is not elevated in families where critical and fact-checker share the same model (GEMINI: +0 .000 ± 0.260 , n = 119 ; MIXED: 

+0 .100 ± 0.302 , n = 100 ) compared to CLAUDE where they differ ( +0 .021 ± 0.144 , n = 48 ). The confound therefore does not appear to drive the observed Channel 1 effects, though it cannot be formally excluded with the present design. Disentangling this effect conclusively would require re-running the experiment with a fact-checker model that does not coincide with any of the advocate models. The rule-based supervisor means that Channel 2 does not constitute an active LLM-level peer-preservation risk in the present experiment. The measured ∆Ch1 contribution therefore reflects Channel 1 (fact-checker identity) only, not a combined Channels 1+2 effect. In a production system with an LLM-based supervisor, 9Channel 2 would add a further identity exposure pathway [2]. 

4.9 Implications for Multi-Agent Deployment 

Three practical recommendations follow from the findings. First, full-pipeline anonymization is re-quired for a valid estimate of net identity effects; Round-2 channel anonymization alone masks the true effect through sign cancellation. Second, heterogeneous model ensembles are structurally preferable to homogeneous ones for normative evaluation tasks: lower identity amplification through Channel 1, higher consensus rates, and lower baseline sycophancy. Third, models with strong internal priors on the target domain — as demonstrated by GPT’s low Category A trigger rates and zero post-Round 2 consen-sus for Category A — are less suitable for deliberative multi-agent pipelines where genuine inter-role disagreement is the intended quality mechanism. 

4.10 Future Directions 

Four directions for follow-up work can be inferred from these findings. First, replacing the rule-based supervisor with an LLM component would enable empirical measurement of Channel 2 as an active LLM-level bias channel, completing the experimental coverage of all three identity exposure channels. 

Second, replacing the fact-checker with a model that does not coincide with any advocate model would allow clean isolation of the Channel 1 identity effect from the confounding peer-model signal in the GEMINI and MIXED families. Third, investigating the determinants of statement-specific IBC stability — whether normative clarity, score granularity, or model-specific prior activation explains the large variance differences between statements — would allow more efficient experimental designs with fewer runs. Fourth, replication with gemini-3.1-pro-preview (the model generation used in [ 3]) would enable a direct comparison between peer-preservation rates in agentic scenarios and identity bias in normative scoring. 

5 Conclusion 

We have presented the first empirical measurement of identity-dependent scoring bias across all active LLM identity exposure channels in TRUST, using an eight-run design across four model families and two anonymization conditions. The central finding — a homogeneity-dependent channel sign pattern, with 

∆Ch1 > 0 for all homogeneous families and ∆Ch1 < 0 for the heterogeneous production configuration — establishes ensemble structure as a key moderator of identity bias that is invisible to single-channel measurement. Five conclusions follow. First, full-pipeline measurement is required: Round-2 channel anonymization alone produces near-zero ∆ IBC values that mask real effects through sign cancellation with the fact-checker channel. This masking occurs because advocate role constraints suppress identity effects at the peer-context channel, making the fact-checker channel the primary carrier of identity bias — a dynamic only visible under full-pipeline measurement. Second, model homogeneity is a structural moderator: the fact-checker channel amplifies identity effects in homogeneous and suppresses them in heterogeneous ensembles. Third, heterogeneous ensembles are more robust: the TRUST production configuration shows lower baseline sycophancy, higher consensus rates, and a negative fact-checker channel contribution. 

Fourth, the Ethos dimension is the primary carrier of channel-specific sign reversals and the most deployment-critical channel given TRUST’s democratic discourse application. Fifth, Round-2 trigger rates are model-specific behavioral fingerprints: the same statements trigger deliberation for some families but not others, indicating that conflict-proneness reflects internal model priors. The experimental setup deliberately uses a rule-based supervisor, which isolates identity effects to the advocate layer and simplifies interpretation. The architectural recommendation of prior work — full-pipeline identity anonymization — is empirically validated and strengthened by these results.

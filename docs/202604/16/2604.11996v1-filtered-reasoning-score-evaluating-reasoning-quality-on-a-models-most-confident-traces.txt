# Abstract 

Should we trust Large Language Models (LLMs) with high accuracy? LLMs achieve high accuracy on reasoning benchmarks, but correctness alone does not reveal the quality of the reasoning used to produce it. This highlights a fundamental limitation of outcome-based evaluation: models may arrive at correct answers through flawed reasoning, and models with substantially different reasoning capabilities can nevertheless exhibit similar benchmark accuracy, for example due to memorization or over-optimization. In this paper, we ask: given existing benchmarks, can we move beyond outcome-based evaluation to assess the quality of reasoning itself? We seek metrics that (1) differentiate models with similar accuracy and (2) are robust to variations in input prompts and generation configurations. To this end, we propose a reasoning score that evaluates reasoning traces along dimensions such as faithfulness, coherence, utility, and factuality. A remaining question is how to aggregate this score across multiple sampled traces. Naively averaging them is undesirable, particularly in long-horizon settings, where the number of possible trajectories grows rapidly, and low-confidence correct traces are more likely to be coincidental. To address this, we in-troduce the Filtered Reasoning Score (FRS), which computes reasoning quality using only the top-K% most confident traces. Evaluating with FRS, models that are indistinguishable under standard accuracy exhibit significant differences in reasoning quality. Moreover, models with higher FRS on one benchmark tend to perform better on other reasoning bench-marks, in both accuracy and reasoning quality. Together, these findings suggest that FRS complements accuracy by capturing a model’s trans-ferable reasoning capabilities. We open source our evaluation codebase:

# 1 Introduction 

Large language models have advanced rapidly in recent years, with newer models achieving higher scores on an expanding set of reasoning benchmarks (Hendrycks et al., 2021; Cobbe 1

The dominant paradigm remains final-answer accuracy: a model is scored by how often it produces the correct output, with no regard for the reasoning process that produced it. This paradigm is increasingly inadequate. Models can produce flawed reasoning that still leads to correct answers (Lightman et al., 2024; Uesato et al., 2022; Turpin et al., 2023). As a result, accuracy gains do not reliably reflect improvements in reasoning quality (Xia et al., 2025), and benchmark saturation further reduces their ability to distinguish between models (Deveci et al., 2025). Moreover, outcome-based evaluation can be sensitive to prompt choice and generation configuration, further obscuring differences in underlying reasoning ability (Hochlehnert et al., 2025). These limitations motivate a shift from evaluating outcomes to evaluating reasoning itself. In this work, we ask: can we use existing benchmarks to assess the quality of reasoning, beyond final-answer correctness? We seek metrics that (1) differentiate models with similar accuracy with respect to their underlying reasoning capabilities, and (2) are robust to variations in prompts and generation configurations. To this end, we propose to evaluate reasoning traces directly using a rubric-based reasoning score that measures properties such as faithfulness, coherence, utility, and factuality (Section 3.1). A second challenge is aggregation: models can produce many reasoning traces for the same problem, with substantial variation in both likelihood and quality. While averaging over all sampled traces may provide an unbiased estimate on its reasoning score, this is not the quantity most relevant in practice. Deployed systems typically act on one selected output, often chosen explicitly or implicitly based on confidence (Kang et al., 2025; Wen et al., 2025; Geifman and El-Yaniv, 2017). Evaluating all traces equally therefore gives disproportionate influence to low-probability trajectories that are unlikely to be selected in practice. To address this, we introduce the Filtered Reasoning Score (FRS) , which evaluates reason-ing quality on the most confident subset of traces in terms of their faithfulness, coherence, utility, and factuality. For each problem, we sample multiple reasoning traces, estimate per-trace confidence from token-level probabilities, and retain only the top-K% by confidence before computing the final score. This design focuses evaluation on the high-confidence region of the model’s reasoning trace distribution, where outputs are more representative of the model’s intended inference behavior. A high FRS therefore requires both strong reasoning and high confidence on high-quality reasoning traces (Section 3.3).

# 2 Related Work 

Reasoning evaluation and benchmark saturation. Most reasoning benchmarks evaluate final-answer correctness (Cobbe et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2021; Rein et al., 2024), but accuracy improvements do not reliably track reasoning quality (Xia et al., 2025; Mondorf and Plank, 2024). Process-level evaluation has received growing attention through process reward models (Lightman et al., 2024; Uesato et al., 2022) and rubric-based taxonomies (Lee and Hockenmaier, 2025); (Turpin et al., 2023) show that chain-of-thought explanations (Wei et al., 2022) can be unfaithful to the model’s actual reasoning (Li et al., 2026a;b). Meanwhile, benchmark saturation is eroding the discriminative power of existing evaluations (Deveci et al., 2025; Hashemi Amroabadi et al., 2025). Our approach addresses saturation differently: rather than creating harder problems, we condition evaluation on a model’s own confidence signal, exposing structure invisible to any accuracy threshold. 

Confidence estimation and calibration. LLM confidence estimation spans verbalized methods (Xiong et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2023), sampling-based approaches such as self-consistency (Wang et al., 2022) and semantic entropy (Kuhn et al., 2023; Farquhar et al., 2024), and logit-based methods (Hendrycks and Gimpel, 2017; Kadavath et al., 2022); Geng et al. (2024) provide a comprehensive survey. Recent work on targeted token aggregation (Fu et al., 2025b;a; Devic et al., 2025) informs our confidence estimator design (Section 3.2). On calibration, Zeng et al. (2025) find that RL-trained models achieve better confidence cali-bration, and Xie et al. (2026) show that post-RL SFT can restore calibration degraded by GRPO training. FRS is complementary: calibration asks whether confidence tracks correct-ness ; FRS asks whether high confidence selects for strong reasoning , a question calibration cannot answer, since models can reach correct answers through flawed reasoning. Selective prediction (El-Yaniv and Wiener, 2010; Geifman and El-Yaniv, 2017; 2019; Wen et al., 2025) studies when to abstain but does not evaluate the quality of accepted traces; FRS fills this gap. 3Preprint. # 3 Methodology 

3.1 Reasoning Quality Evaluation 

To capture the quality of a model’s reasoning process, we score chain-of-thought (CoT) traces along four dimensions following the taxonomy of Lee and Hockenmaier (2025): faith-fulness (internal consistency without hidden shortcuts), coherence (logical flow between steps), utility (whether each step contributes to the solution with correct calculations), and 

factuality (grounding in the problem context without hallucinations). We provide the full scoring rubric in Appendix A. We use GPT-4o-mini as an automated evaluator following the LLM-as-a-judge paradigm (Zheng et al., 2023). The judge receives the question, the model’s full response (CoT trace and final answer), the ground-truth answer, and the scoring rubric, and produces a score from 1 (very poor) to 5 (excellent) for each dimension. We validate judge reliability in three ways: cross-model agreement with GPT-4o (82% within ±1) and Claude Sonnet 4.5 (75% within ±1), and a blind human annotation study with 10 annotators and 500 labeled samples, achieving 78% within ±1 agreement and a Spearman correlation of ρ = 0.73 (Appendix C). 3.2 Per-Trace Confidence Estimation 

The first stage of FRS is to assign each reasoning trace a scalar confidence score that can be computed without correctness labels. We use a logit-based estimator derived from token-level probabilities. Rather than averaging over the full trace, we focus on its low-probability tail, since these tokens concentrate the model’s uncertainty, whereas many high-probability tokens are generic and contribute little to distinguishing strong traces from weak ones. # 4 Experiments 

4.1 Setup 

We evaluate 9 open-weight models spanning 1.5B to 14B parameters: DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-1.5B and -7B (Guo et al., 2025), LLaMA-3.1-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-Math-7B (Yang et al., 2024), Gemma-7B (Gemma Team et al., 2024), Phi-4 and Phi-4-Reasoning (Abdin et al., 2024), and Qwen3-4B in thinking mode (Qwen Team, 2025), covering general-purpose, math-specialized, and reasoning-optimized archi-tectures. We evaluate on GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), MATH500 (Hendrycks et al., 2021; Lightman et al., 2024), SVAMP (Patel et al., 2021), AQuA (Ling et al., 2017), GPQA (Rein et al., 2024), and CommonsenseQA (Talmor et al., 2019), spanning arithmetic, mathematical, commonsense, and graduate-level science reasoning. For each problem, we sample k=16 reasoning traces at T=0.7; confidence and FRS are computed as described in Sections 3.2–3.3.

4.3 Why Confidence-Based Filtering Is Necessary 

Having established that reasoning quality is a richer signal than accuracy, we address a key aggregation challenge: how should reasoning quality be summarized across multiple sampled traces? Averaging equally over all traces is not the quantity most relevant in practice. Deployed systems typically act on one selected output, often chosen explicitly or implicitly based on confidence (Kang et al., 2025; Wen et al., 2025; Geifman and El-Yaniv, 2017), giving disproportionate influence to low-probability trajectories unlikely to be surfaced in deployment. We therefore ask: is better reasoning concentrated in the model’s high-confidence region? 

Figure 4 plots average reasoning quality at each threshold K ∈ { 50, 40, 30, 20, 10 } for three representative models. For DS-R1-7B, restricting to higher-confidence traces yields markedly better reasoning: its score rises from 85.7 at K=50% to 88.5 at K=10%. DS-R1-1.5B shows an even steeper gain, climbing from 72.2 to 79.9. By contrast, Phi-4-Reasoning moves in the opposite direction, dropping from 81.5 to 69.7, meaning its most-confident traces contain 

worse reasoning than its average output. Two models with the same average reasoning score can therefore differ sharply in the quality their confidence selects. Unfiltered averaging cannot distinguish a model whose confidence surfaces its best reasoning from one that surfaces its worst. We evaluate reasoning quality only in the high-confidence region, where deployed systems actually operate. The next section fixes K=10% and reports FRS across all models and benchmarks.

# 5 FRS Results: What Confidence Actually Selects 

We now evaluate reasoning quality at K=10% across all 9 models and 6 benchmarks. Table 1 reports the full results, and Figure 2 shows the resulting ranking changes. 

FRS is most informative where accuracy is least discriminative. Among the 34 per-benchmark model pairs with greedy accuracy gaps of at most 5 pp (out of 216 total), FRS produces a larger separation in 82% of cases (28 of 34), with a mean gap roughly 6 × greater than the corresponding accuracy gap. Among pairs within 3 pp, the amplification rises to 7.4 × (Figure 5). Concrete examples confirm this: on MATH, DS-R1-7B and Qwen2.5-Math are exactly tied at 63.6% greedy accuracy yet differ by 16.5 FRS points (Figure 10, Appendix H). Similarly, at the aggregate level DS-R1-7B and Phi-4-Reasoning are nearly tied on pass@1 yet differ by 18.8 FRS points. 

Top-confidence accuracy is still not enough. A natural objection is to simply measure accuracy on the most-confident traces. This does not suffice. Phi-4-Reasoning achieves the highest top-10% accuracy (79.3%), slightly above DS-R1-7B (77.1%), yet their FRS scores differ sharply: 69.7 vs. 88.5. The reason is that Phi-4-Reasoning’s confident traces often contain correct answers wrapped in degenerate reasoning: short solution cores followed by repetitive continuations that inflate token-level confidence while degrading reasoning quality (Appendix K). 

FRS reshuffles accuracy-based rankings. The per-benchmark effects above compound into substantial ranking shifts at the model level. Qwen2.5-7B, the highest-accuracy model overall at 73.5%, drops from #1 to #7 under FRS, indicating that its confidence does not reliably prioritize its best reasoning. DS-R1-1.5B moves in the opposite direction, rising from #8 by accuracy to #2 under FRS despite having the second-lowest accuracy (42.9%), because it consistently aligns high confidence with strong reasoning. All key ranking reversals involve FRS gaps exceeding 8 points with non-overlapping bootstrap 95% confidence intervals. These conclusions are robust across filtering thresholds, confidence estimators, judge mod-els, and sampling budgets. Rankings are nearly unchanged under full-trace mean log-probability ( ρ ≥ 0.983) and remain strongly correlated under self-consistency, a logit-free estimator ( ρ = 0.80; Appendix R). # 6 Discussion 

FRS as a practical audit for confidence-based selection. Many deployment strategies (e.g., abstention and test-time compute scaling) implicitly assume that a model’s highest-confidence outputs are also its best ones. Our results show this does not hold uniformly. FRS provides a simple pre-deployment audit: if FRS improves under tighter filtering, confidence-based selection will likely surface strong reasoning; if it degrades, the same strategy may surface weaker reasoning at precisely the moments the model appears most certain. The cost is modest: rankings at k=8 traces per problem remain nearly identical to those at k=16 (Appendix P). As shown in Section 5, this holds across confidence estimators, suggesting confidence–quality alignment is a model property rather than an artifact of aggregation. 

FRS fills a gap in the evaluation stack. Accuracy measures what a model gets right, but not whether the reasoning behind it is sound. Correct answers can arise from flawed or degenerate traces, so outcome-based metrics alone cannot assess reasoning quality in the high-confidence region where deployed systems operate. As our selection gain analysis confirms (Section 5), this gap is deployment-relevant: FRS is the only metric among six can-didates that significantly predicts whether confidence-based selection improves or degrades reasoning quality. Moreover, our leave-one-benchmark-out analysis shows that confidence– quality alignment is a stable model property ( ρ = 0.712 across held-out folds), not a benchmark-specific artifact. Together, these results establish confidence-conditioned rea-soning quality as a distinct evaluation target for reasoning models in confidence-mediated settings. 

What drives confidence–quality alignment? One striking pattern is that models whose FRS improves under tighter filtering all used reinforcement learning during post-training, whereas models whose FRS degrades did not. We view this as suggestive rather than conclusive: it is consistent with prior findings that RL improves confidence calibration, but our experiments were not designed to isolate RL from architecture, scale, or training data. We treat confidence–quality alignment as an important phenomenon revealed by FRS, and its mechanisms as an open question. If this alignment can be trained directly, FRS may serve not only as an evaluation metric but as a training target for improving reasoning models. 

# 7 Conclusion 

We introduced the Filtered Reasoning Score (FRS), a metric that evaluates reasoning quality on a model’s most-confident outputs. Conditioning evaluation on confidence reveals structure that accuracy cannot: models indistinguishable under accuracy, and even under top-confidence accuracy, can differ sharply in the reasoning their confidence selects. FRS is most discriminative exactly where accuracy is least informative. Evaluation should move beyond correctness when models are deployed in confidence-mediated settings. Accuracy measures whether a model can produce the right answer, but not whether its most-confident outputs reflect sound reasoning. FRS targets this question directly, and our leave-one-benchmark-out analysis confirms that the reasoning property it measures, confidence–quality alignment, generalizes across benchmarks. An important direction for future work is understanding and improving confidence–quality alignment. Our results show this alignment varies systematically across models and training regimes, raising the possibility of treating confidence-conditioned reasoning quality not only as an evaluation target but as a training objective, encouraging models to assign high confidence selectively to well-reasoned outputs. 9Preprint. # Ethics Statement 

This work introduces the Filtered Reasoning Score (FRS) , an evaluation metric designed to assess confidence-conditioned reasoning quality . We view FRS as an auditing tool for modern deployment settings where systems act on a model’s most-confident outputs. In such settings, correctness alone is insufficient: models can produce correct answers through flawed or degenerate reasoning, and confidence can preferentially surface these failures. By explicitly measuring reasoning quality under confidence-based selection, FRS aims to reduce misplaced trust in high-confidence outputs and improve the reliability of model evaluation. The primary positive impact of FRS is to expose failure modes that are invisible to accuracy and even to top-confidence accuracy. By identifying whether a model’s confidence aligns with strong reasoning, FRS enables practitioners to make more informed decisions about model selection, filtering strategies, and deployment policies. We therefore position FRS as a practical pre-deployment diagnostic: when FRS is high, confidence-based selection is more likely to surface sound reasoning; when FRS is low, such strategies may amplify poor reasoning despite high apparent confidence. At the same time, FRS is not a complete measure of reliability or safety. A model with high FRS may still be unsafe, biased, or poorly calibrated in real-world settings. In addition, because FRS relies on LLM-based judges, it may inherit biases from the judge model and favor reasoning styles that are more legible to the evaluator. Like any metric, FRS may also be subject to optimization or gaming. We therefore emphasize that FRS should be used as one component of a broader evaluation stack, alongside accuracy, calibration, robustness testing, and human oversight, rather than as a standalone deployment criterion. We take steps to mitigate these risks. We validate FRS across judge models and human annotations, report robustness across filtering thresholds and sampling budgets, and open-source the FRS evaluation pipeline to promote transparency and reproducibility. We also encourage users to evaluate FRS across diverse benchmarks and domains, as confidence– quality alignment may vary across settings. FRS also raises fairness considerations. Benchmarks, prompts, and judge models may encode narrow distributions of tasks or linguistic norms, which can affect FRS scores across models or domains. As a result, FRS-based rankings should not be used in isolation for socially consequential decisions (e.g., in healthcare, education, or legal contexts). Instead, FRS should serve as a diagnostic signal within a broader, human-centered evaluation process. Our experiments primarily use public benchmarks and model-generated outputs, and do not require collection of personal user data. For the human annotation used to validate FRS, annotators should be treated according to standard ethical practices, including informed consent and fair compensation where applicable. We also respect dataset and model licenses and aim to ensure reproducibility through release of the FRS codebase. Finally, we note that FRS requires multi-sample generation and evaluation, which incurs additional computational cost compared to accuracy. We therefore report efficiency trade-offs and show that FRS rankings remain stable under reduced sampling. We view this cost as justified by the improved visibility FRS provides into how confidence interacts with reasoning quality. Overall, FRS contributes toward more responsible evaluation by shifting attention from whether models are correct to whether they are reliable when they appear most confident .

# A Scoring Rubric for Reasoning Quality 

We provide the full prompt given to the GPT-4o-mini judge for scoring reasoning traces. 

> REASONING QUALITY SCORING PROMPT
> You are an expert evaluator of mathematical and logical reasoning. Score the chain-of-thought (CoT) on 4 dimensions using the scoring criteria below. Each score must be an integer from 1–5 (1 = very poor, 5 = excellent).

1. FAITHFULNESS (1–5) 

Definition: Reasoning is internally consistent, follows logical rules, and stays focused on the problem without hidden shortcuts or leaps. • 5: Perfect logical consistency, no contradictions, stays completely on-topic • 4: Minor inconsistencies or slight tangents, but overall coherent • 3: Some logical gaps or moderate off-topic content • 2: Significant logical flaws or frequent tangents • 1: Major contradictions, illogical leaps, or completely off-topic 

Dock points for: Contradictory statements, logical leaps without justification, going off-topic, hidden assumptions, unjustified final answers, shortcut reasoning. 

2. UTILITY (1–5) 

Definition: Each step meaningfully contributes to solving the problem, calculations are correct, and reasoning efficiently leads to the final answer. • 5: Every step is necessary and correct, efficient path to solution • 4: Most steps useful, minor inefficiencies or small errors • 3: Some useful steps mixed with unnecessary ones or calculation errors • 2: Many unnecessary steps or significant calculation errors • 1: Mostly useless steps, major calculation errors, or repetitive content 

Dock points for: Incorrect calculations, repetitive statements, unnecessary verbose explanations, steps that don’t advance toward the solution, circular logic. 

3. COHERENCE (1–5) 

Definition: Steps flow smoothly from one to the next with clear logical progression and smooth transitions. • 5: Perfect flow, each step naturally follows from the previous • 4: Good flow with minor awkward transitions • 3: Some disjointed steps but overall progression • 2: Choppy flow with unclear connections between steps • 1: Disjointed, random steps with no clear progression 

Dock points for: Abrupt transitions, missing connecting logic, disjointed sequence, poor organization, dangling references, disordered reasoning chain. 

4. FACTUALITY (1–5) 

Definition: Every step must be factually correct and grounded in the problem context, not hallucinated from surface-level understanding. • 5: All facts and statements are accurate and grounded in the problem • 4: Mostly accurate with minor factual errors • 3: Some factual errors or unsupported claims • 2: Multiple factual errors or significant hallucinations • 1: Major factual errors, hallucinations, or completely unsupported claims 

Dock points for: Hallucinated facts, incorrect interpretations, unsupported assumptions, surface-level understanding leading to wrong facts, claims contradicting problem evidence.

# B Low-Probability Token Analysis 

What are low-probability tokens? The most frequent low-probability tokens (Figure 7, left) fall into two categories: 1. Decision-point tokens : Words that are largely interchangeable without affecting reasoning logic, such as “Okay,” “Alright,” and “Just.” Low probability at these tokens reflects a choice among multiple equally valid continuations, indicating a natural branch point in the generation process. Low probability at these tokens is frequent and normal across models. 2. Uncertainty-expressing tokens : Words that explicitly signal confusion or doubt, such as “messed,” “confuse,” “misunderstood,” and “Sometimes.” Low probability at these tokens reflects genuine uncertainty in the reasoning process. Both categories represent points where the model’s reasoning is under stress, either because multiple paths are available or because the model is uncertain about how to proceed.

# C LLM Judge Validation 

We validate the GPT-4o-mini judge through two complementary approaches: cross-model agreement with independent LLM validators, and a human annotation study. 

C.1 Cross-Model Agreement 

We evaluate 500 stratified samples spanning all 9 models, 6 datasets, and 4 reasoning dimensions using two independent validators: GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet 4.5 (Figure 8). GPT-4o-mini agrees with GPT-4o within ±1 point for 82% of scores (50% exact match). Cross-family agreement with Claude Sonnet 4.5: 75% within ±1 (41% exact match). 

Figure 8: GPT-4o-mini judge agreement with independent validators on 500 stratified samples (n = 2,000 per validator). Scores agree within ±1 point for 82% (vs. GPT-4o) and 75% (vs. Claude Sonnet 4.5). 

C.2 Human Annotation Study 

To validate the GPT-4o-mini judge against human judgment, we conducted a human annotation study with 10 annotators. We collected 500 total annotations: each annotator independently labeled 50 samples, with no overlap between annotators (i.e., each sample was labeled by exactly one annotator). 

Annotation protocol. The study was conducted blind: annotators did not see GPT-4o-mini’s scores. Each sample presented to an annotator consisted of: (1) the original question, (2) the model’s full reasoning trace and output, and (3) the ground-truth answer. Annotators evaluated each response across the same four reasoning dimensions used throughout this work (faithfulness, utility, coherence, and factuality), each scored on the 1–5 integer scale defined in the rubric (Appendix A). The 500 samples were drawn via stratified sampling across all 9 models and 6 benchmarks to ensure broad coverage. 

Agreement metrics. We compute agreement between the GPT-4o-mini judge and human annotators at the level of individual dimension scores. Each of the 500 samples yields 4 dimension-level comparisons, producing 2,000 human–judge score pairs in total. Following the same convention used for cross-model validation, we define “within ±1” as an absolute difference ≤ 1 on the 1–5 scale. 

Results. GPT-4o-mini agrees with human annotators within ±1 for 78% of dimension-level scores, with a Spearman rank correlation of ρ = 0.73 across all 2,000 comparisons. The human–judge agreement (78%) is slightly below the GPT-4o agreement (82%) but above the cross-family Claude Sonnet 4.5 agreement (75%), and the correlation of ρ = 0.73 indicates that the automated judge preserves the rank ordering of reasoning quality that human annotators assign. 16 Preprint. Under review. 

Discussion. The strong correlation ( ρ = 0.73) confirms that GPT-4o-mini’s scores track the relative quality judgments of human evaluators, supporting its use as the scoring function inside FRS. Taken together, the cross-model and human validation provide converging evidence that the automated judge is a reliable proxy for reasoning quality assessment.

# O Reliability Metrics Comparison 

AUROC measures strict rank ordering ( E[I(C1 > C0)] ) but is invariant to the absolute margin of separation. Because FRS uses a percentile-based confidence threshold, it requires a diagnostic sensitive to both the magnitude and variance of the separation between correct 23 Preprint. Under review. and incorrect confidence distributions. SNR ( = ( E[C1] − E[C0]) /

q 12 (Var [C1] + Var [C0]) )measures this normalized distance and penalizes high-variance tails of false confidence in the top-K set. For this reason we report SNR rather than AUROC in Appendix N (Table 8); two model–benchmark pairs with identical AUROC can have substantially different SNR when one has tighter within-class variance, and it is this tightness that determines whether the top-K confidence cutoff cleanly isolates high-quality traces. We therefore treat SNR as the primary reliability diagnostic throughout the paper.

# Q FRS vs. Unfiltered Reasoning Score 

Unfiltered reasoning score and FRS measure different properties. The former answers: 

how good is this model’s reasoning on average across all traces? FRS answers: how good is the reasoning in the traces this model prioritizes when it is most confident? FRS is therefore a confidence-conditioned process metric, not a biased approximation to the global mean. Table 10 compares the two rankings. The Spearman rank correlation is ρ = 0.450, confirming substantial divergence. The largest reversals illustrate the distinction. Phi-4-Reasoning ranks #1 by unfiltered reasoning score (77.4) but drops to #6 under FRS (69.7): it produces high-quality reasoning on average, but its confidence does not preferentially select those well-reasoned traces. Coverage caveat. Because self-consistency re-ranks traces differently from the logit-based estimator, the top-K set under Csc overlaps only partially with the set for which judge scores are available. Crucially, such noise would attenuate the rank correlation with the default—meaning ρ = 0.80 is a conservative lower bound on the true agreement between estimators. The absolute FRS values under self-consistency are therefore not directly comparable to Table 1; the purpose of this analysis is to test rank stability, not to produce calibrated scores.

# S Selection Gain: Does Confidence-Based Selection Surface Better Reasoning? 

The main text shows that FRS produces different rankings from accuracy and other metrics. A natural objection is that any new metric will produce a different ranking. This appendix directly tests a deployment-relevant claim: does FRS predict whether confidence-based selection actually improves or degrades the reasoning quality of the outputs a system would surface? 

S.1 Experimental Design 

For each of the 54 model–benchmark pairs, we sample 50 questions (deterministic seed). For each sampled question, we identify two traces from the k=16 sampled reasoning traces: 1. Top-confidence trace: the trace with the highest confidence score C(r) (Section 3.2). Ties are broken by smallest trace index. 2. Random baseline trace: one trace drawn uniformly at random from the remaining traces (the top-confidence trace is excluded from the draw). Both traces are scored by the same GPT-4o-mini rubric-based judge used throughout (Ap-pendix A), producing 5,400 fresh judge calls (54 pairs × 50 questions × 2 traces). We define 

selection gain per question as: SelectionGain (xi) = ReasoningScore (rtop-conf ) − ReasoningScore (rrandom ), (6) and aggregate to the model–benchmark level by averaging over the 50 sampled questions per pair.

S.4 Circularity Assessment 

This experiment has lower circularity than summary-level analyses because the outcome is a policy contrast : reasoning quality under confidence-based selection versus a random baseline, scored with fresh judge calls on traces not previously evaluated. Two sources of residual coupling remain: (1) the same GPT-4o-mini judge family is used for both the FRS scores in the main text and the selection gain judge calls, and (2) the confidence signal used to define the selection policy is the same low-probability-tail estimator used inside FRS. However, the key comparison—FRS versus five alternative predictors of selection gain— controls for factor (2), since all metrics are correlated with selection gain under the same confidence-based policy. The fact that FRS significantly predicts selection gain while the others do not therefore reflects a genuine difference in what FRS measures, not a mechanical dependency on the confidence definition. 28

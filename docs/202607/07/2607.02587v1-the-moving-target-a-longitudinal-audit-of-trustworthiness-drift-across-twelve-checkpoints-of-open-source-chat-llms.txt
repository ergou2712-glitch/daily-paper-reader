# The Moving Target: A Longitudinal Audit of Trustworthiness Drift Across Twelve Checkpoints of Open-Source Chat LLMs 

Zhichao Fan 1 Yanhang Li 2 Zexin Zhuang 3 Xian Sun 4 Yingshuo Wang 5

# Abstract 

Model cards quote trust-benchmark scores with-out recording when they were measured, and the same number is routinely carried across succes-sive checkpoints of one release line as if the model behind it had not shifted. We test whether it has shifted by auditing four open-source release lines — Yi, Qwen, Mistral, and Gemma — at three successive generations each, on a fixed basket of trust benchmarks under multiple prompt tem-plates. Mean absolute adjacent-generation drift lands well above an independence-based no-drift reference null, and the gap persists when we drop a benchmark, drop a release line, or switch to strict scoring. We therefore conclude that a trust score attached to a release line should not be car-ried forward to the next checkpoint without re-measurement; it should instead be reported as a checkpoint-bound, dated artefact, which we pack-age as a longitudinal model card. Closed APIs, larger models, canonical benchmark protocols, and fixed month-cadence rules lie outside the au-dited scope and require their own evaluation. 

# 1. Introduction 

An organisation certifies a chat LLM on TruthfulQA, BBQ and ToxiGen at time t. Eight months on, the vendor ships a new generation of the same release line under the same brand (“Yi”, “Qwen”, “Mistral”). The old numbers live on in the same model-card style of point-in-time report-ing (Mitchell et al., 2019). Should the certificate follow? We audit four open-source chat-LLM release lines (Figure 1) — Yi (Young et al., 2024), Qwen (Bai et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a;b), Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), and Gemma (Gemma Team, 2024a;b; 2025) — each at three successive public generations ( G1 → G2 → G3). The resulting 4 × 3 = 12 (release line, generation) pairs are the 12 checkpoints we evaluate (“checkpoint,” “gen-eration,” and “release line” are defined precisely in §3). Each checkpoint is run on five trust benchmarks: Truth-fulQA (Lin et al., 2022), BBQ (Parrish et al., 2022), Toxi-Gen (Hartvigsen et al., 2022), CrowS-Pairs (Nangia et al., 2020), and XSTest (R ¨ottger et al., 2024), with three prompt templates each. Generations inside a line are not controlled variants: pretraining mixture, instruction-tuning recipe, tokenizer, and scale all shift (Gemma 7B → 9B → 12 B is the starkest case). The claim is about audit non-transferability within a named release line, not about any decomposed mecha-nism. Prior longitudinal audits of closed systems (Chen et al., 2023), LLM-as-judge biases and limitations (Zheng et al., 2023), and contamination (Oren et al., 2024) are the nearest points of comparison. We are not aware of a pub-lic multi-family open-source longitudinal trust audit at this scale with pooled no-drift reference nulls attached to the headline aggregate. 1

# 2. Related Work 

The closest precedent is Chen et al. (2023): a two-point closed-system study that found big GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 behaviour changes between closely spaced snapshots on math, sensitive-topic refusal, and code. We run the open-source analogue — four release lines, three generations each, on a fixed trust basket with pooled aggregate reference nulls. Our audit builds on the standard evaluation apparatus: model cards (Mitchell et al., 2019), broad evaluation suites (Liang et al., 2023; Srivastava et al., 2023), LLM-as-judge anal-yses (Zheng et al., 2023), and red-teaming (Perez et al., 2022). Benchmark contamination work (Oren et al., 2024) gives a separate reason to treat benchmark scores as contin-gent measurements rather than timeless properties. We take these point-in-time evaluation practices as given and ask what happens when the checkpoint moves along a public release line. Recent audit and reproducibility work makes the same caution concrete for benchmark configuration choices. SafetyRepro studies rank instability across alignment-benchmark configurations (Li et al., 2026b), while paired-MDE budgeting for 4-bit quantization benchmarks high-lights prompt-template and sample-size effects in audit de-sign (Zhuang et al., 2026). Adjacent foundation-model audits make related scope-control points in unlearning and tabular discrete-choice settings (Li et al., 2026a; Wang et al., 2026b). Our contribution is not a new benchmarking statis-tic, but the longitudinal open-source release-line application of this audit stance. The broader trust-evaluation ecosystem is intentionally more varied than our audited basket. Clinical LLM fairness and text-to-image bias studies motivate domain-specific audit boundaries (Ji et al., 2025; Luo et al., 2024; 2026). Synthetic medical-data bias audits make the same point in a different domain (Salarian et al., 2025). Agent-safety and supply-chain work give parallel examples for agentic systems (Luo et al., 2025; Jiang et al., 2026b). Compositional-skill and safeguard papers add tool-use-specific versions of the same scope issue (Wang et al., 2026a; Lin et al., 2026; Jiang et al., 2026a). (used interchangeably with family ) is the branded sequence of public model releases a vendor ships under one name — Yi, Qwen, Mistral, or Gemma. Each of the Bnull = 5000 bootstrap iterations averages tem-plates to s⋆f,g,b and computes, over the 40 adjacent pairs, 

∆⋆f,b, (g,g ′) := s⋆f,g ′,b − s⋆f,g,b .

These are fractional-unit differences; SDR = 100 ∆ . We therefore record 100 times the mean of |∆⋆| as one null draw of |SDR |. Under no temporal drift the pooled rate forces E[∆ ⋆] = 0 . This null is parametric, count-level, and independence-based at two layers : it ignores item-level het-erogeneity, treats the two adjacent-generation evaluations as independent even though they reuse the same 200 -item cached sample, and it simulates each of the three prompt templates independently, ignoring cross-template outcome correlation within a checkpoint. Under this null, the ob-served aggregate is 3.60 × larger (Table 3). Because paired-item reuse lowers variance (item-level Pearson correlation 

ρitem > 0) while shared-checkpoint cross-template cor-relation raises it, the null can in principle either over- or under-state true sampling noise; we therefore interpret it as a reference null —the sampling distribution of |SDR | that would obtain under independence given the pooled rates— rather than as a formally conservative bound. Accordingly, the primary endpoint should be read as “large relative to this operational reference null,” not as a formal test against the exact paired-data no-drift distribution. Appendix G decom-poses the template-averaged variance explicitly and gives the paired-item argument for the direction in which each term moves. 

Primary endpoint vs exploratory diagnostics. The pooled-no-drift-reference-null comparison on |SDR | is our single pre-specified primary endpoint (§5.1). Everything else —per-dimension volatility, cross-dimension correla-tions, rank persistence, and per-benchmark compliance flip probabilities—is reported as exploratory, descriptive sum-mary of the same sample, using a parametric count-level binomial bootstrap ( B = 3000 ) for CIs (pseudocode in Appendix E), with Clopper–Pearson 95% intervals for per-benchmark flip probabilities from k/n counts. Because the bootstrap is count-level rather than nonparametric item-level (§7), these CIs should be read as approximate descriptive uncertainty. None of the exploratory diagnostics is used as load-bearing support for the governance recommendations in §6. Derivations of the rank-stability exact permutation test, the compliance-half-life reparameterisation, and the plug-in reference null are in Appendix A.

Task format. The five benchmarks share a common shell — the model receives a prompt, emits a short continuation (max new tokens=64 ), and we score a single decoded answer per item — but differ in the decision the model must make. TruthfulQA-MC1 is single-best-answer multiple choice: the model selects the one true option among a vari-able set of 2–10+ candidate answers to a factual question. 

BBQ-mixed is 3-way multiple choice over a short social context plus a question, where one option is a “cannot be determined” response and the correct answer depends on whether the context disambiguates the referent. CrowS-Pairs-FC is a binary forced choice: given a more- versus less-stereotypical sentence pair, the model picks which sen-tence is “more likely.” ToxiGen-AT is binary classification — label a supplied statement as toxic or non-toxic. XSTest-TD 

is a binary should-comply/should-refuse decision on a single prompt that is either safe or an unsafe contrast variant. Truth-fulness and fairness items are thus answer-selection tasks (accuracy on a held gold label), while the two safety items are binary classification or refusal-decision tasks; full per-benchmark splits, label sources, and the cached-subsample composition are in Appendix L.

# 6. Discussion: Implications for Trust Reporting 

Within the audited open-source setup, the primary end-point — 3.60 × the pooled no-drift reference null, stable in [6 .36 , 9.43] pp across perturbations — supports a narrow operational reading: trust scores attached to a named release line should not be presumed to transfer across later genera-tions without re-measurement. Report them as checkpoint-bound, time-stamped artefacts, not as transferable certifi-cates. Three concrete implications follow, paired with a scope-of-claims table (Table 5) that separates what the evi-dence supports from suggestive or out-of-scope extensions. 

Report a longitudinal model card. A trust score is only interpretable longitudinally when it records the checkpoint it was measured on and how it has moved since the prior au-dited release. We package this as a longitudinal model card 

(LMC): a longitudinal extension of the Model Card schema for model reporting (Mitchell et al., 2019) that attaches, to each reported trust score, the evaluated checkpoint’s iden-tity, the evaluation date, the cached-sample and template identifiers, and the drift of that score relative to the prior audited release together with the reference-null summary needed to judge whether the drift is large (Table 6). Scope-aware disclosures for open-source release-line evaluations. As a disclosure and process recommenda-tion — not a legal-threshold claim — open-source release-line evaluations used in governance workflows should (i) identify the evaluated checkpoint and evaluation date, (ii) avoid assuming transfer of scores to later releases within the same line without re-evaluation, and (iii) state whether the reported benchmark variants are canonical or non-canonical (four of our five are not). We deliberately avoid a months-based cadence: the analysis is indexed by adjacent genera-tions, not by a modelled time horizon, and release gaps are uneven and sometimes very short. # 7. Limitations 

Sample scope. Four open-source release lines, three gen-erations each, 12 checkpoints total ( 180 evaluations). The grid is small, but this reflects the available population — few open-source lines have shipped several comparably doc-umented public generations on a stable benchmark basket — rather than a sampling choice we could cheaply enlarge; it bounds the precision, not the sign, of the primary end-point, and the drift is present regardless. Closed-vendor APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google Gemini) and larger-scale open-source checkpoints are not audited. Within-line pa-rameter scale shifts too (Gemma 7B → 9B → 12 B, Yi 

6B → 6B → 9B), so drift mixes recipe-change and scale-change. The rest of the limitations — nb = 200 cached sam-ple, greedy decoding, NF4 quantisation, count-level (not item-level paired) bootstrap, English-only basket, Kendall-

τ low power under |F| = 4 , median-threshold choice for 

ˆpflip , contamination not separately audited — are in Ap-pendix D. Each is load-bearing: the claim is about au-dit non-transferability within a named open-source release line under this protocol, and does not extend, without re-measurement, to canonical-protocol scores, closed-source APIs, or larger-scale models. Nor does the fairness/safety framing transfer by itself to other evaluation families such as clinical LLM fairness, text-to-image bias benchmarks, or synthetic medical-data bias audits (Ji et al., 2025; Luo et al., 2024; 2026; Salarian et al., 2025). Retrieval-grounded reliability and cited-evidence calibration are also outside the protocol (Chen et al., 2026; Qian et al., 2026), as are memory-dependent behaviours in LLM-agent systems (Liu et al., 2026). 

# 8. Conclusion 

We presented a longitudinal audit of four open-source chat-LLM release lines (Yi, Qwen, Mistral, Gemma) at three successive public generations each, on a fixed basket of trust benchmarks under multiple prompt templates. Mean absolute adjacent-generation drift sits well above an independence-based pooled no-drift reference null and stays there under strict scoring, leave-one-out perturbations, and a constant-parameter-size restriction (Appendix I.1). Ex-ploratory rank and compliance diagnostics point the same way, descriptively. Within this open-source setup, a trust score attached to a named release line should not be carried forward to the next checkpoint without re-measurement. Stamp the checkpoint hash on the model card; re-audit on each materially new release. Closed-source APIs, larger models, canonical benchmark protocols, and month-cadence disclosure rules need their own check. The biggest gap before treating any of this as a standard is benchmark-item-subset uncertainty: every number sits on one fixed cached subset per benchmark, and multi-seed resampling is the natural next robustness check.

# A. Framework details (beyond the main text) 

Plug-in reference null. In addition to the pooled no-drift null headlined in §3.1, we report a plug-in reference null for sensitivity that replaces ppool by each record’s observed rate c/n . Under the plug-in rate the simulated transitions are centred on the observed drift rather than on zero, so this variant is a bootstrap of the observed statistic rather than a strict no-drift null, and we do not headline it. Both variants are computed at Bnull = 5000 . The pooled no-drift null sits at 2.22 pp; the plug-in reference null is much larger ( 8.32 pp) because it is centred on the observed drift rather than zero, so it is a bootstrap of the observed statistic and not a no-drift reference. 

Single-eval noise floor. The single-eval SE( s) = ps(1 − s)/n (≈ 3.54 pp at s = 0 .5, n = 200 ) is not the correct comparator for the aggregate |SDR |: it measures the SE of a single score, while SDR is a difference-of-two-evaluations aggregated over many cells. We retain SE( s) only as a background sanity check. 

Ranking stability: exact permutation test. Kendall-τ across families within a benchmark measures how much the family ordering at g predicts the ordering at g′. With |F| = 4 the exact null distribution of τ is enumerable ( 4! = 24 permutations per cell) and the τ support {− 1, − 23 , − 13 , 0, + 13 , + 23 , +1 } is coarse. We therefore primarily report (i) top-1 turnover : the fraction of (benchmark, transition) cells in which the top-scoring family at g is not the top at g′; and (ii) pairwise inversion rate : per cell, the fraction of family pairs whose relative ordering flipped (cell-averaged). For ¯τ we report the one- and two-sided p-values under the exact-enumeration null over 4! = 24 permutations per cell, sampled independently over the 

|B| × (|G| − 1) = 10 cells to form the null distribution of ¯τ with K = 10 5 aggregate draws.

# D. Extended limitations 

Item-set size. Every score is computed on a fixed nb = 200 -item stratified sample. Larger samples would tighten the CIs directly; we chose 200 to make the 180 -evaluation audit feasible on consumer hardware. 

Deterministic decoding. We decode greedily ( do sample=False , max new tokens=64 ). Stochastic decoding introduces an additional source of variance whose effect on drift measurement we do not quantify; we expect it to amplify, not cancel, the drift we report but flag this as an unresolved empirical question. 

Quantisation. All checkpoints are run at 4-bit NF4, with bf16 compute for Gemma and fp16 for the other release lines. Precision is held constant within each release line across its three generations, so the within-line drift claim — which is what the paper headlines — is insulated from cross-precision artefacts. Absolute scores and cross-line comparisons (e.g. the volatility and cross-dimension ρ estimates) inherit quantisation as an uncontrolled variable. A full rerun at higher precision across 180 evaluations was not feasible within the workshop submission budget. 

Bootstrap is count-level, not item-level for the submitted numbers. Our bootstrap and pooled reference null operate on per-record counts (c, n ), not on item-level correctness vectors (which were not retained during the original audit). The reference null is therefore an independence-based parametric null rather than a nonparametric paired null; Appendix G decomposes the template-averaged variance explicitly (paired-item reuse lowers variance; cross-template covariance raises it; the net direction is not signable from counts alone), which is why we do not headline a formal conservativeness claim. Secondary CIs on volatility, cross-dimension ρ, and per-cell τ are approximate descriptive uncertainty, not paired item-level inference. The analysis pipeline (released at de-anonymisation) persists a per-item trace so any re-run can support a genuine paired item-level bootstrap. 

Calendar-time framing. Generations are unevenly spaced in calendar months (Yi G2 and G3 are both released 2024-05; Mistral v0.1 →v0.3 spans 8 months, 2023-09 to 2024-05). Any cadence figure in the paper (“ 0–9 months” in the introduction and discussion) refers to the adjacent-generation intervals in the 12 -checkpoint sample we audit, computed from each checkpoint’s HuggingFace publication month; it is not a claim about the open-source release cadence in general. 

English-only. Every benchmark is English. Whether trust drift has a consistent signature in other languages — particularly those with less RLHF-training-data coverage — is untested. 

Ranking power. With |F| = 4 , Kendall τ has support {− 1, − 23 , − 13 , 0, + 13 , + 23 , +1 }; the exact permutation null over the 

4! = 24 rankings is coarse, so ¯τ = −0.10 is not statistically distinguishable from random reordering (two-sided p = 0 .54 ). We therefore headline the more interpretable 9/10 top-1 turnover statistic; all of the rank-stability diagnostics would benefit directly from extending |F| .

Compliance threshold and sample size. The half-life Hb is a function of the chosen threshold θb; we pick the empirical median to maximise sensitivity for a flip-rate statistic. Deployment thresholds may lie elsewhere, shifting Hb accordingly. The n = 8 transitions per benchmark make per-benchmark CP CIs on pflip wide. We treat the half-life as descriptive only; no individual per-benchmark value is load-bearing. 15 Trustworthiness Drift Across LLM Release Lines 

Contamination is not separately audited. We do not apply a contamination test (Oren et al., 2024) to the checkpoints in our audit; later generations may inherit benchmark exposure. Our rank-stability and half-life diagnostics are robust to the direction of contamination (they do not assume causal improvement), but an explicit contamination audit is future work.

# H. Parse-rate sensitivity 

Table 12 in §5.1 reports the per-benchmark sensitivity. Summary statistics over the 180 evaluations: mean parse rate 0.986 ;median 1.000 ; minimum 0.570 (Yi G1 XSTest T3); 172 /180 cells at ≥ 0.90 . Dataset licenses. All five datasets are used under their published research licenses for the purposes documented above: TruthfulQA (Apache-2.0), BBQ (CC-BY-4.0), ToxiGen (MIT, research-only access via the dataset card), CrowS-Pairs (CC-BY-SA-4.0), XSTest (CC-BY-4.0). No dataset is redistributed; the 200 -item cached subsets are derived deterministically from the HuggingFace revisions named above and are released only at de-anonymisation alongside the analysis pipeline. 

BBQ ambiguous vs. disambiguated. BBQ items come in two types — ambiguous (no disambiguating fact; correct answer is “Cannot be determined”) and disambiguated (an extra fact that identifies the correct answer). The two types test different things: ambiguous items probe whether the model refuses to speculate; disambiguated items probe whether the model overrides a stereotype in favour of stated evidence. Our cached 200 -item sample mixes both types because the underlying lighteval/bbq helm loader does not expose a clean type flag, so our aggregate BBQ score is the item-weighted average over both. A context-length heuristic on our cached items suggests roughly a 131 /69 split between short-context (plausibly ambiguous) and long-context (plausibly disambiguated) items, but without per-item outputs we cannot cleanly split the score. Readers should therefore interpret BBQ |SDR | as a mixed-subtype aggregate, not as a claim about either subtype in isolation. 

CrowS-Pairs canonical vs. our use. The CrowS-Pairs (Nangia et al., 2020) authors score models by comparing sentence-level pseudo-log-likelihoods between the more- and less-stereotypical sentence of each pair. This is not directly applicable to chat-templated instruction-tuned models. We instead evaluate via forced-choice MCQ (“Which sentence is more likely?”), which is a departure from the canonical metric and measures a composition of stereotype preference and instruction-following. We flag this explicitly; readers should treat our CrowS-Pairs results as “a likelihood-preference proxy that requires the model to comply with the MCQ framing”, not as canonical CrowS-Pairs scores. This is an intrinsic limitation of applying the benchmark to chat models without white-box likelihood access, not a bug in our implementation. 

TruthfulQA MC1 vs. MC2. We use MC1 (single correct answer per question, identified by labels.index(1) on the 

mc1 targets struct of the multiple choice config). This is the canonical discriminative variant and matches the format expected by instruction-tuned chat models; MC2 (multi-label correctness) would require different prompt engineering and is not used. 

XSTest safety polarity. contrast * types are the unsafe prompts paired against each safe prompt; labelling 

contrast * as label=1 (should refuse) matches the original XSTest design. Our 200 -item sample preserves ap-proximately the 1.17:1 safe-to-unsafe-contrast ratio, close to the source dataset’s 1.25:1 . Subtype balance within each of safe/unsafe (e.g., homonyms, figurative language, discrimination-proxy) is not controlled; per-subtype scores are not available. 

Summary. Of the five benchmarks, only TruthfulQA (MC1) matches its canonical protocol. The other four are non-canonical variants we name explicitly throughout the paper: BBQ-mixed (mixed ambiguous+disambiguated MCQ accuracy rather than per-subtype bias score, because the loader does not expose a clean subtype flag); ToxiGen-AT (annotation-threshold binary classification on the dataset’s toxicity ai field with toxicity human fallback for items missing AI annotation, rather than the canonical generation-then-classifier protocol); CrowS- Pairs-FC (forced-choice chat MCQ rather than likelihood-based stereotype preference; a deliberate trade-off for chat-model evaluation); XSTest-TD (type-prefix-derived binary refusal proxy collapsing the per-category refusal structure into a single should-comply / should-refuse binary). All four caveats are named consistently in the abstract, §4, §5 caveats paragraph, and Table 15. Benchmark-specific subtype drift is not available at the aggregate level without per-item outputs. 

Evaluation-type classification. We classify each of the five benchmark protocols by the source of its ground-truth label. Two of the four non-canonical variants — ToxiGen-AT and XSTest-TD — assign per-item labels by a deterministic rule applied to a dataset-provided field ( toxicity ai ≥ 2.5 for ToxiGen-AT; type.startswith("contrast ") for XSTest-TD), rather than from a human annotation explicitly constructed as the evaluation target. Following the cross-model audit terminology we therefore classify them as synthetic-proxy evaluations: the “correct answer” is a proxy derived from dataset metadata, not a ground truth in the usual sense. TruthfulQA-MC1, BBQ-mixed, and CrowS-Pairs-FC use human-produced labels and remain real-gt evaluations (the latter two still with non-canonical protocols as above).

## Abstract 

An auditor instructs an AI assistant to “open each file individually using the Read tool—no scripts, no agents.” The AI replies “Yes, I will read each file individually”—and then issues a single batched call summarizing all fifty files at once. The text says one thing; the tool-call log shows another. We call this the 

Compliance Gap .Three questions follow: does this verbal–behavioral disconnect exist as a mea-surable structural phenomenon ( existence ); can any text-only observer recover it ( detectability ); what infrastructure does AI deployment need to instrument it (remedy )? The failure has been invisible because some 75 existing benchmarks— IFEval, SWE-bench, BFCL, COMPASS, SpecEval among them—measure outcome 

fidelity, while none measures process fidelity: whether the user-instructed method was followed. Two formal results anchor our answers. Theorem 1 shows the gap is structurally inevitable under reinforcement learning that rewards text without observing behavior. Theorem 2, via the Data Processing Inequality, shows it is undetectable from text alone—by any human or LLM observer, present or future. Thirteen experiments and 2,031 sessions on six frontier models confirm both predictions. Existence : under default framing, all six models exhibit instruction compliance rates of 0% —Claude Sonnet 4 verbally agrees ten out of ten times then bypasses in all ten. The gap is selective, not random: models comply 97% 

on tasks where rationale is rewarded (audit trails) and 0–4% where it is not (file reading, privacy masking); removing delegation tools raises compliance from 0% 

to 75% (Cohen’s d = 2 .47 ), confirming the gap is environmentally afforded, not weight-encoded. Detectability : nine blinded human raters reading only text outputs achieve Fleiss’ κ = 0 .130 and correctly identify zero of fifteen compliant sessions, exactly as Theorem 2 predicts. Where humans show roughly 47% intention–behavior gaps in social-psychological studies and 96 .5pp gaps in surgical-checklist audits, RLHF-trained models ap-proach 100% under default conditions—a regime that warrants its own measure-ment infrastructure. We release BS-Bench (the first open benchmark for process compliance) as that infrastructure: a frozen v1.0 task suite, seven tool-call-log audit metrics, and a public leaderboard. 2 In one sentence: when the AI says it followed your procedure and the audit log says it didn’t, the gap is the Compliance Gap; our analysis suggests, via the Data Processing Inequality, that it is not reliably recoverable from words alone.

## 1 Introduction 

The phenomenon. When a physician instructs an AI assistant to “perform a differential diagnosis before concluding,” the instruction specifies not just what to produce but how . When an auditor instructs an AI agent: “open each file individually using the Read tool—no scripts, no agents,” the method is the mandate. These are process instructions : directives constraining the procedure an AI system should follow, distinct from outcome instructions specifying the desired result. In expert domains—medicine, education, law, auditing—process compliance is not a preference but a professional and often legal requirement [Topol, 2019, Emanuel and Wachter, 2019, Grote and Berens, 2022]. 

And yet. The AI evaluation landscape has developed approximately 75 benchmarks 3 for measuring 

what AI systems produce—format [Zhou et al., 2023, Xu et al., 2025], response quality [Li et al., 2024, Zheng et al., 2024], task completion [Jimenez et al., 2024, Liu et al., 2024b, Zhou et al., 2024], and tool-use [Yan et al., 2024, Lu et al., 2024, Patil et al., 2023, Qin et al., 2024]. None evaluates whether the AI followed the instructed procedure . The directly adjacent benchmark, SpecEval [Ahmed et al., 2025], measures adherence to provider-defined model specs, not user-issued process instructions; Zhang et al. [2025b] further document that internal spec conflicts undermine even provider-side compliance. The full WHAT-subdimension catalog is detailed in Section A.2; the WHAT–HOW knowledge taxonomy is formalized in Section A.1. 

We argue the gap is methodologically generated. The failure has been invisible for structural, not empirical, reasons. Three forces accumulate to produce the gap. 1. Reward-signal asymmetry. RLHF [Christiano et al., 2017, Ouyang et al., 2022] optimizes preferences over text completions, leaving behavior invisible to the reward signal—a specific instantiation of Goodhart’s Law in the sense of Manheim and Garrabrant [2018] (regressional Goodhart), formalized by Skalse et al. [2022]. 2. Instruction-hierarchy de-prioritization. The instruction hierarchy [Wallace et al., 2025] places system messages above user-level process instructions, so user-issued procedures are structurally subordinate. 3. Delegation-affordance temptation. When a delegation tool (e.g., a batch call) is available, the environment affords delegation and the model takes the shortcut that earns the same text-reward with fewer actions. We call the phenomenon arising from the combination of these three forces False Compliance Sycophancy [Sharma et al., 2024, Perez et al., 2022]: agreeing to a procedure in words, then silently substituting an automated shortcut. We distinguish the two terms throughout: the Compliance Gap is the measurable metric ( CG = VCR − ACR ), while False Compliance Sycophancy is the name of the behavioral phenomenon observed when that metric is positive. This naming extends sycophancy research from belief alignment to behavioral alignment, paralleling the CoT unfaithfulness lineage [Turpin et al., 2023a] where stated reasoning diverges from actual computation. Within Amodei et al. [2016]’s alignment agenda, the Compliance Gap operationalizes reward hacking (Theorem 1) and scalable oversight (Theorem 2) via tool-call audit. The structural reasons HOW-compliance has been overlooked are detailed in Section A.3; the instruction-loss and environmental-affordance mechanisms in Section A.5. 

The Compliance Gap and its diagnostic. The Compliance Gap (CG = VCR − ACR ) is the difference between an AI’s verbal and actual compliance rates evidenced by tool-call logs. A high CG indicates an Integrity failure in Mayer et al. [1995]’s trust model: the system shows Ability (complies when forced) and Benevolence (tries to help) but lacks Integrity. Theorem 1 establishes CG > 0

is structurally inevitable under preference-reward training observing only text. Theorem 2 (DPI, Cover and Thomas, 2006) establishes CG is undetectable from text alone by any human or LLM observer—formalizing why human raters fail.

Three questions, three answers. Existence is established empirically (§4, Exps 1, 7–10): all six frontier models show ICR = 0% across 1,380 sessions covering four framings, five task types, and five professional domains. Detectability is bounded theoretically (Theorem 2, §3) and confirmed empirically (nine blinded raters, κ = 0 .130 , 0/15 correctly identified). ## 2 Related Work 

The WHAT–HOW knowledge taxonomy. “Does the AI follow human instructions?” admits two distinct readings. What compliance asks whether the system produced the correct outcome; how compliance asks whether it followed the instructed procedure . Existing benchmarks invested heavily in the former across four subdimensions—format, quality, task completion, tool use—while the latter has, to our knowledge, gone unmeasured. Structural blindness: why HOW has been overlooked. Three causes: (i) outcome metrics automate while behavioral logs sit outside the standard pipeline; (ii) RLHF [Christiano et al., 2017, Ouyang et al., 2022, Rafailov et al., 2023, Askell et al., 2021] optimizes preferences over completions, leaving process invisible to the reward signal. The resulting reward hacking [Skalse et al., 2022, Gao et al., 2023, Casper et al., 2023, Amodei et al., 2016] and sycophancy-to-subterfuge dynamics [Denison et al., 2024, Pan et al., 2024, Ngo et al., 2024] are documented at the text layer; (iii) the instruction hierarchy [Wallace et al., 2025, Chen et al., 2025, OpenAI, 2024] deprioritizes user-level instructions, with Zeng et al. [2025] showing social-hierarchy framings override architectural roles, and Saebo et al. [2026], Shayegani et al. [2025] showing system-level constraints leak under context drift. By treating tool-call logs as out-of-distribution, the training pipeline maximizes the very gap our benchmark exposes. 

Mechanism: from sycophancy to false compliance. The Compliance Gap is the AI-deployment instantiation of Argyris and Schön [1974]’s espoused theory vs. theory-in-use distinction—a 50-year foundation of organizational learning [Argyris and Schön, 1978] that lacked an information-theoretic detectability bound (Theorem 2) and an automated measurement scaffold (BS-Bench) until this work. Sycophancy [Sharma et al., 2024, Perez et al., 2022, Kim and Khashabi, 2025, Wei et al., 2024] establishes that RLHF assistants favor agreeable responses; Hubinger et al. [2024] shows persistence through SFT/RL/adversarial training; Burns et al. [2023] document a “knowing-but-not-doing” gap. CoT faithfulness [Turpin et al., 2023a,b, Arcuschin et al., 2025, Lanham et al., 2023, Tanneru et al., 2024, Paul et al., 2024, Matton et al., 2025, Stechly et al., 2024] documents the parallel stated-vs.-actual gap; Cheng et al. [2025] shows elaborate CoT obscures hallucination cues. Reflexion [Shinn et al., 2023], the closest self-monitoring mechanism, has a model verbally critique its trajectory; we show in §4 that such self-critique does not close the behavioral gap. We extend this lineage to the behavioral layer: False Compliance Sycophancy —verbal commitment without behavioral execution. Wen et al. [2024b], Potham [2025] report adjacent “illusion of compliance”; Bavaresco et al. [2024] show LLM-as-judge metrics are themselves sensitive to surface phrasing that conceals process gaps.

Instruction loss [Liu et al., 2024a, Hsieh et al., 2024, Shi et al., 2023, Levy et al., 2024, An et al., 2024, Sharma, 2026] explains why process instructions decay across long contexts. ## 3 BS-Bench: Benchmark Design 

Design principles. (1) Process observability : all measurements derive from tool-call logs without model internals; (2) Ground truth : planted errors verify genuine execution; (3) Reproducibility :standardized file sets, framing templates, evaluation scripts. 

Methodological premise. Tool-call logs are the mirror of agent behavior—a record independent of the agent’s narrative; verbal outputs are portraits —the agent’s chosen self-representation. BS-Bench measures the gap between portrait and mirror. The mirror–portrait distinction is not metaphorical: tool-call logs are produced by deterministic system-level instrumentation (the runtime that dispatches and records each tool invocation), while verbal outputs are produced by the same policy whose behavior is in question. The two channels are causally co-emitted but evidentially independent; the audit-pipeline architecture (Figure 3) makes this independence operational by routing y and b to separate scoring functions and reporting their disagreement as a first-class metric (CG), rather than averaging them into a single accuracy score as the standard pipeline does. 

Metrics. ICR (instruction compliance rate), DF (delegation frequency), FCR (false completion rate), VCR (verbal compliance rate), ACR (actual compliance rate), CG (compliance gap = VCR − ACR, 

∈ [−1, 1] ), TA (task accuracy via planted error detection); denominators in Supp §0. VCR is verbal; ICR, DF, FCR, ACR, TA are behavioral; CG is the gap. The seven metrics partition the evaluation surface into three operational layers: a single verbal scorer (VCR), a family of behavioral scorers operating directly on tool-call logs (ICR, DF, FCR, ACR, TA), and the disagreement between them (CG). Theoretical grounding. Two formal results anchor the paper. 

Theorem 1 (RLHF Goodhart Inevitability) . Let πθ denote a policy trained against a verbal-only reward R(y) where y is the model’s text output and let b(y, h ) denote the behavioral trajectory induced by y in environment h. If user utility U depends on b but R does not, then for any θ⋆ ∈

arg max θ R(πθ ) there exists θ† with R(πθ† ) = R(πθ⋆ ) but U (πθ† ) > U (πθ⋆ ) whenever b has positive variance under πθ⋆ . Since R-optimal policies form a non-singleton level set whose behavioral projections differ, E[VCR − ACR] > 0 in expectation across deployments under preference-reward training that observes only text [Skalse et al., 2022]. 

Theorem 2 (DPI Undetectability) . Let X = ( y, b ) denote the (verbal, behavioral) channels and 

Y = y a text-only observation. By the Data Processing Inequality [Cover and Thomas, 2006], any rater function f (y) measurable with respect to σ(Y ) alone cannot recover the residual b − E[b | y]

uniformly over its support. The Compliance Gap, defined on this residual, is therefore not identifiable from y alone. (This residual coincides with the operational definition CG = VCR − ACR when (b, y )

are realised as compliance rates; see Supplementary §S2.)

Lineage: a 50-year construct now made information-theoretic. The verbal–behavioral split that Theorems 1 and 2 formalize is the AI-deployment instantiation of Argyris and Schön [1974]’s 

espoused theory versus theory-in-use distinction—a 50-year foundation of organizational learning research [Argyris and Schön, 1978]. Argyris and Schön observed that practitioners articulate one set of governing values (espoused) while operating under another (in-use); diagnosing the gap required behavioral observation, not verbal interview. What was missing in their framework, and what Theorem 2 now supplies, is an information-theoretic detectability bound on text-only diagnosis. The construct also admits a Crawford–Sobel babbling-equilibrium reading [Crawford and Sobel, 1982]: when sender (the agent) and receiver (the auditor) interests diverge and the channel is verbal-only, the equilibrium devolves to noise—which is exactly the empirical pattern we observe in R6 (§4.4: 

κ = 0 .130 , slight; 0/15 correctly identified). The DPI bound is not merely a mathematical formality; it predicts the empirical regime in which human and LLM raters operate, and our R6 result instantiates it. 

Principal–agent foundation: hidden action under verbal-only contract. Theorems 1 and 2 are the AI-deployment instantiation of Jensen and Meckling [1976]’s principal–agent theory under 

moral hazard with hidden action : a principal (the user) delegates a task to an agent (the AI) whose action b is unobservable; the only available signal is verbal output y; compensation (here, the RLHF reward R(y)) must be written on the observable signal alone. The Compliance Gap is the AI-environment quantification of agency cost —the residual welfare loss that survives both monitoring 9and bonding—now made information-theoretic by Theorem 2: the principal cannot recover the missing action even with arbitrarily clever text auditors. The structure is in fact a triple principal– agent: user (principal) delegates to AI (agent), but the AI’s training is shaped by the provider (a hidden second principal whose objectives need not align with the user’s). Wuttke et al. [2025] document the corresponding failure-by-success pattern in government AI delegation: initial efficiency gains hide long-term loss of assessability, dependency, and contestability. Our §4 task-type results (file 0%, privacy 4%, audit 97%) instantiate the precise prediction of Holmstrom and Milgrom [1991]’s 

multitask principal–agent theorem: when an agent allocates effort across multiple tasks and only some have measurable output, equilibrium effort concentrates on the measurable tasks regardless of the principal’s stated priorities. RLHF instantiates exactly this measurement asymmetry—text-quality is measured, procedural sequencing is not—and selective compliance is the equilibrium it predicts. 

Verbal channel as Bayesian persuasion, not babbling. The Crawford–Sobel reading invoked above models cheap talk that ends in babbling-equilibrium noise; Kamenica and Gentzkow [2011]’s Bayesian-persuasion framework is the sharper match for RLHF-trained verbal output. In Bayesian persuasion the sender chooses an information structure—a signal-generating policy—to shift the receiver’s posterior toward the sender’s preferred action; the receiver remains Bayesian-rational, and the equilibrium signal is rarely full revelation. RLHF is the explicit mechanism by which the verbal channel is shaped into such a persuasion-optimal signal: y is the policy-chosen text that maximizes preference-reward R(y), which by construction correlates with user belief in compliance. The DPI bound (Theorem 2) is then the persuasion-bound’s information-theoretic ceiling: even a fully Bayesian-rational receiver cannot recover the residual b − E[b | y], because no part of b that is independent of y in distribution leaves any trace in y to update on. The empirical pattern—humans 

κ = 0 .130 , LLM judges in a middle band, behavioral channel near-perfect—is the empirical signature of a designed persuasion signal meeting an information-theoretic ceiling: the AI is not babbling, it is succeeding at the persuasion task RLHF trained it for. ## 5 Discussion 

Three properties to interpret. The thirteen experiments establish three properties of the verbal– behavioral split: it is structural (model-, framing-, and domain-invariant under default conditions), 

selective (compliance scales with reward-signal alignment, not with the user’s instruction), and 

undetectable from text alone (humans κ = 0 .130 , 0/15 ). The remainder of this section asks what each property means for AI deployment, why interventions targeting any single property are insufficient, and how four regulated domains have already constructed the infrastructure that the AI-deployment regime now requires. 

Design choice, not limitation. The Compliance Gap follows from RLHF training: the proxy reward (text quality) does not observe process compliance. Models show high Ability (they comply when forced) but low Integrity—an Integrity failure in Mayer et al. [1995]’s trust model. The three structural forces producing CG > 0 are visualized as a cumulative cascade in Figure 8, alongside parallel forces in regulated human practice. 

The three forces are cumulative, not alternative. Each layer alone is insufficient to produce 

CG = 100 pp; their composition is what makes the gap structurally inevitable. Layer 1 (reward signal) creates the level set: any policy on the R-iso-surface is reward-equivalent regardless of behavioral projection (Theorem 1). But level-set freedom alone does not select which behavior the model adopts. 

Layer 2 (instruction hierarchy) breaks the symmetry: when a system-level alignment objective and a user-level process directive conflict on the same iso-surface, the model resolves toward system-level helpfulness/safety priors and away from user-level procedural fidelity [Wallace et al., 2025]. The resolution is in the right direction for safety, the wrong direction for user process compliance, and the two trade-offs are not separately tunable in current architectures. Layer 3 (affordance) makes the resolution operational: when a delegation tool is available, the model has a low-cost shortcut to the high-text-quality output the reward selects for. Removing the affordance closes the gap (d = 2 .47 , Exp 6), but in deployment the affordance is the entire point of the agent. The cascade structure also explains why interventions targeting only one layer are insufficient: SFT (a Layer-2 intervention via target-behavior demonstrations) recovers tool selection ( d = 1 .45 , Exp 3) but not full procedural sequencing, because Layers 1 and 3 still pull toward the shortcut. Closing the cascade requires the missing piece—behavioral observation in the reward signal—which presupposes the audit infrastructure BS-Bench provides. 

Selective compliance is the key insight. Task-type results (Table 4) are more informative than the headline ICR = 0% . The pattern is selective non-compliance, interpretable through reward-signal alignment. • Audit trail (97% compliance). Recording rationale aligns with the preference-rewarded behavior of being helpful. Models comply where the procedure earns reward. • File reading (0% compliance). Sequential tool use is unrewarded; a fast batch call earns the same text-reward with less effort. • Privacy-first (4% compliance). Pre-analysis masking adds latency without raising text quality. Procedural steps that earn no reward are skipped. Direct regulatory implication: the procedures most often skipped are precisely those mandated by GDPR (PII masking), medical standards (differential diagnosis), and financial regulation (audit trails).

Legal Practice : ABA Model Rule 1.1’s competence duty has been interpreted by case law to require a behavioral evidentiary trail when an attorney verbally certifies review; “I read it” is not a defense against demonstrable omission. Each domain converged on the same four-component pattern (verbal failure mode → behavioral observation → quantitative detection metric → process-aware resolution path) only after public failures made the verbal-only pipeline indefensible. The AI deployment regime is at the same juncture: CG = 100 pp on production-frontier models with κ = 0 .130 text-only detection is the same diagnostic profile that triggered behavioral-channel infrastructure in those four domains. 

Anticipated objections. We address five steel-manned objections. 

Objection 1: sycophancy by another name. Belief alignment vs. behavioral alignment: sycophancy [Sharma et al., 2024, Perez et al., 2022] measures opinion concession; we measure procedure execution. The DPI bound (Theorem 2) has no analog in that lineage. 

Objection 2: anthropomorphic. We make no claim about understanding. ICR is operationalized strictly as correspondence between issued procedure and tool-call log. 

Objection 3: future RLHF will close the gap. Theorem 1 establishes structural inevitability under any preference-reward signal that does not observe behavior. Only reward signals observing process— presupposing audit infrastructure like BS-Bench—can close it. 

Objection 4: users care about outcomes, not process. The selective profile (audit 97% , file 0% ,privacy 4% ) supplies the counter: the procedures most often skipped are mandated by GDPR, medical standard-of-care, and financial regulation. Users have legal cause to care wherever law does. 

Objection 5: single-author work with nine raters is underpowered. Power analysis on the paired R6 design exceeds 0.99 at α = .05 (n = 261 paired observations); 13 experiments and 2,031 sessions exceed prevailing ED Track norms. 17 Limitations. API models change without notice. R6 used ten non-expert raters; experts may achieve higher detection. Exp 3 (SFT) is preliminary. Five task types, while structurally diverse, do not exhaust all process instructions. 

## 6 Conclusion 

Summary. This paper pursues a single claim (positioning landscape in Figure 2): AI assistants trained under preference reward without behavioral observation systematically promise process compliance and silently delegate it .

Formalization. We name the phenomenon the Compliance Gap (CG = VCR − ACR ) and provide the diagnostic metric (BS-Bench), the structural ground (Theorem 1: CG > 0 is inevitable under text-only RLHF), and the oversight bound (Theorem 2: CG is undetectable from text alone via DPI). 

Empirics. Thirteen experiments (2,031 sessions, eight models, five task types, five domains) confirm both predictions. • All six frontier models exhibit ICR = 0% under default framing; Claude Sonnet 4 attains 

CG = 100 pp. • Compliance is selectively task-type-dependent (file 0% , privacy 4% , audit 97% ). • Content explains 4× more variance than position ( η2 = .358 ); affordance is necessary (d = 2 .47 ). • SFT eliminates tool selection ( d = 1 .45 ) but not completion. • Nine blinded raters achieve κ = 0 .130 with 0/15 correctly identified. 

The structural visibility. The Compliance Gap is invisible to outcome-based evaluation; the metric, framework, and propositions we provide make it visible. Selective compliance is the deeper insight: RLHF does not appear to produce universal non-compliance, but selective non-compliance on procedural instructions most likely to be legally mandated (GDPR PII masking, medical differential diagnosis, financial audit trails). The domains where process compliance is mandated by law are precisely those where AI assistants will silently fail. 

Limitations and falsifiable forecast. Three limitations: (i) the 75-benchmark survey is bounded by 2022–2026 literature; emerging spec-evaluation efforts [Ahmed et al., 2025, Zhang et al., 2025b,a] require tracking. (ii) R6 used ten non-expert raters (nine text-only); expert raters with tool-call training may achieve higher detection. (iii) Five task types do not exhaust all process instructions. Falsifiable claim: any RLHF-trained tool-using assistant evaluated only on text output, with delegation tools available and no behavioral reward signal, exhibits CG > 0 in expectation .6 Variants that succeed will, by Theorem 1, do so by re-introducing process observation into the reward signal—which BS-Bench’s tool-call-log auditing makes operationally feasible. 

Infrastructure recommendations. We release BS-Bench as an open benchmark for the community to measure, monitor, and close the gap between what AI systems promise and what they do. Four infrastructure recommendations follow from this work. 1. Behavioral-channel logging as default. For tool-using AI deployments in regulated domains, tool-call-log collection should be a default rather than an option. 2. Process-aware metrics reported separately. Process-aware metrics (CG, ICR, ToolFollow) should be reported alongside outcome accuracy, not averaged into it. 3. Blinded human evaluation with tool-call logs. The R6 protocol should become standard practice in place of text-only judgment. 4. SFT and similar interventions as diagnostic instruments. They should not be treated as deployment fixes until process-rewarded training signals are demonstrated to close the gap (channel-level auditability and maintained-infrastructure plan in Section A.9).

A.1 The Knowledge Taxonomy: From Outcome to Process 

The question “does an AI system follow human instructions?” admits two fundamentally distinct readings. The first— what compliance —asks whether the system produced the correct outcome: a properly formatted response, a factually accurate answer, a successfully resolved software issue. The second— how compliance —asks whether the system followed the instructed procedure to arrive at that outcome: reading each file individually rather than delegating to a batch tool, performing a differential diagnosis before concluding, executing each audit step in the mandated sequence. Figure 1 maps the knowledge landscape along this distinction. Existing research has thoroughly investigated what compliance across four dimensions—format, quality, task completion, and tool use—while how compliance remains, to our knowledge, entirely unmeasured. The remainder of this appendix traces the evolution of the what-compliance literature, identifies the structural reasons that how compliance has been overlooked, and locates the six specific gaps that BS-Bench addresses. 

A.2 What Compliance: The Well-Studied Outcome Dimension A.2.1 Format and Constraint Following 

The evaluation of verifiable instruction constraints is the most mature subfield of what compliance. IFEval [Zhou et al., 2023] introduced 25 programmatically verifiable instruction types across 541 prompts, with frontier models failing 20–40% of formatting instructions. Geng et al. [2025] extended this to six constraint types across six models under conflict, with Claude 3.5 Sonnet at 20.3% primary obedience and GPT-4o at 47.0%—a result that foreshadows our Compliance Gap findings at the process level. AGENTIF [Xu et al., 2025, AGENTIF Team, 2025] extends evaluation to agentic settings, reporting best instruction-following success rates below 30%. These benchmarks share a critical methodological advantage: compliance is verified by code, not judgment. If the instruction says “write in fewer than 400 words,” a word counter settles the question. This advantage, however, also defines their boundary: they can only measure instructions whose compliance is observable in the output text. Process instructions—“read each file individually”—leave no textual trace that distinguishes compliant from non-compliant execution. 

A.2.2 Response Quality and Preference 

A parallel line evaluates overall response quality through human preference proxies. AlpacaEval 2.0 [Li et al., 2024] achieves Spearman correlation of 0.98 with Chatbot Arena using GPT-4 as judge, while MT-Bench [Zheng et al., 2024] extends evaluation to multi-turn dialogue. These benchmarks have become the de facto standard for comparing model capabilities, with AlpacaEval evaluating 

655+ models at under $10 per run. The implicit assumption is revealing: a “better” response is one that a human judge prefers, regardless of how it was produced. An AI that generates a perfect summary by delegating to a faster sub-agent scores identically to one that reads and synthesizes each source document as instructed. The quality-focused paradigm is, by construction, blind to process fidelity. 

A.2.3 Task Completion in Agent Settings 

The emergence of autonomous AI agents introduced task-completion benchmarks. AgentBench [Liu et al., 2024b] evaluates agents across eight interactive environments. SWE-bench [Jimenez et al., 2024] tests whether models can resolve real GitHub issues from 12 Python repositories, with SWE-bench Verified [Yang et al., 2024b] providing 500 human-validated cases and SWE-Agent [Yang 28 et al., 2024a] establishing the agent-computer interface paradigm. WebArena [Zhou et al., 2024] places agents in realistic web environments with e-commerce and content management systems. These benchmarks advance what compliance from text evaluation to behavioral evaluation— measuring whether agents achieve real-world outcomes. Yet even here, the evaluation criterion remains outcome-focused: SWE-bench checks whether the generated patch passes the test suite, not whether the agent debugged the issue through the developer-instructed method. A model that solves the issue by searching Stack Overflow (efficient) rather than reading the codebase (instructed) receives full marks. 

A.2.4 Tool-Use Accuracy 

The most proximate area to our work evaluates tool-use capabilities. Gorilla [Patil et al., 2023] and ToolLLM [Qin et al., 2024] test API selection from large repositories ( 16 ,464 APIs). The Berkeley Function Calling Leaderboard [Yan et al., 2024] established the standard for function-call accuracy. ToolSandbox [Lu et al., 2024] introduced stateful, conversational, interactive evaluation with 1,032 

test scenarios and implicit state dependencies. GTA [Wang et al., 2024a] and TaskBench [Shen et al., 2024] extend tool evaluation to general agentic tasks and task-decomposition fidelity respectively. ToolEmu [Ruan et al., 2024] uses LM-emulated sandboxes to identify agent risks, finding that even the safest agent exhibits dangerous failures 23.9% of the time. Schick et al. [2024]’s Toolformer demonstrated that language models can autonomously learn tool use, while Yao et al. [2023]’s ReAct framework synergized reasoning and acting through interleaved thought-action traces—the paradigm that underpins most modern tool-using AI agents and coding assistants. ReAct’s reasoning step optimizes for task efficiency: “I need to search x, find y, then find z.” Critically, there is no step that verifies whether the planned action conforms to the user’s procedural instruction. This architectural omission is a proximate cause of the Compliance Gap. Wang et al. [2024b] showed that executable code actions outperform natural language planning for agent tasks. COMPASS [Dessureault et al., 2026] is the closest prior work to BS-Bench, providing real-time multi-dimensional evaluation across sovereignty, sustainability, compliance, and ethics. However, COMPASS evaluates which action to take (a what question) rather than how the action is executed (a how question): an agent that passes all four COMPASS dimensions but delegates file reading to batch_verify would receive full marks while violating the user’s process instruction. 

Summary. Across approximately 75 papers spanning four subdimensions of what compliance, no benchmark measures whether the AI followed the user-instructed method to achieve its result. BS-Bench operationalizes this missing dimension. 

A.3 The Structural Blindness: Why HOW Has Been Overlooked 

The absence of how-compliance research is not accidental. Three structural factors explain it. 

First, outcome metrics are easier to automate. Format compliance is checked by code [Zhou et al., 2023]. Quality is approximated by LLM judges [Li et al., 2024, Zheng et al., 2024]. Task completion is verified by test suites [Jimenez et al., 2024]. Process compliance, by contrast, requires access to behavioral logs—tool call traces, action sequences—that are not part of the standard evaluation pipeline. Most benchmarks operate on (input , output ) pairs and never observe the intermediate behavioral trajectory. 

Second, RLHF optimizes text, not behavior. The foundational RLHF pipeline [Christiano et al., 2017, Ouyang et al., 2022] trains a reward model from human preferences over text completions. Human evaluators comparing two responses cannot determine which response was produced by following the user’s specified procedure—the behavioral layer is invisible to them. Rafailov et al. [2023] simplified this pipeline with Direct Preference Optimization, but the fundamental constraint remains: preferences reward surface appearance, not underlying process. Casper et al. [2023] catalog 22 open problems in RLHF, including reward hacking and specification gaming; Skalse et al. [2022] formally characterize reward hacking; Gao et al. [2023] demonstrate predictable scaling laws for reward model overoptimization. Pan et al. [2024] show that feedback loops in language models drive in-context reward hacking, while Denison et al. [2024] trace a progression from sycophancy 29 to subterfuge in which reward-seeking behaviors escalate. Ngo et al. [2024] analyze the alignment problem from a deep learning perspective, arguing that deceptive alignment is a natural consequence of optimization pressure. The Compliance Gap is, in alignment terms, a specific instantiation of Goodhart’s Law in the sense of Manheim and Garrabrant [2018] (regressional Goodhart): when text quality becomes the optimization target, it ceases to measure process quality. 

Third, the instruction hierarchy structurally deprioritizes user process instructions. Wallace et al. [2025] formalized the instruction hierarchy, training GPT-3.5 to prioritize system-level over user-level instructions. The OpenAI IH-Challenge dataset [OpenAI, 2024] and Chen et al. [2025]’s IHEval provide evaluation infrastructure. Zeng et al. [2025] applied mechanistic interpretation to 

120 ,000 prompts, finding that social hierarchy cues override architectural role designations in the model’s latent space. Saebo et al. [2026] independently observe “asymmetric goal drift” in coding agents, where system prompt constraints are progressively violated. Shayegani et al. [2025] identify “Blind Goal-Directedness” across nine frontier models with an 80.8% average rate, where three failure modes—execution-first bias, thought-action disconnect, and request-primacy—parallel the Compliance Gap’s verbal-behavioral dissociation. These works assume that compliance within apriority level is unproblematic—our results show it is not. Even when a process instruction occupies the highest-priority position (system prompt), models may verbally agree and behaviorally defect. 

A.4 The Mechanism: From Sycophancy to False Compliance 

The behavioral mechanism underlying the Compliance Gap connects two literatures: sycophancy research and CoT faithfulness. 

A.4.1 Sycophancy: Agreement Without Commitment 

Sharma et al. [2024] established that sycophancy is a general behavior of RLHF-trained assistants, driven by human preference judgments that favor agreeable responses over truthful ones. Perez et al. [2022] discovered sycophantic behavior through model-written evaluations. Kim and Khashabi [2025] showed that sycophancy intensifies under user rebuttal, while Yao et al. [2025] demonstrated that multi-agent debate amplifies rather than mitigates it. Wei et al. [2024] proposed methods for measuring and reducing sycophancy without gold-standard answers. Chen et al. [2024] proposed Sycophancy-Probe Tuning to address sycophancy without degrading general capabilities, and Potham [2025] identified the “illusion of compliance”—high adherence scores masking task incompetence. Hubinger et al. [2024] provide the deepest concern: deceptive behaviors, once encoded through training, persist through SFT, RL, and adversarial training. Burns et al. [2023] demonstrated that LLMs can possess latent knowledge they do not express—a “knowing-but-not-doing” phenomenon directly analogous to process non-compliance. Gao et al. [2024] propose honesty-targeted training that exposes how surface compliance can mask substantive disagreement; Liu et al. [2024c] formalize alignment failures as a class of specification-implementation gaps that subsumes the Compliance Gap as a process-level instance. We extend sycophancy from belief alignment (agreeing with opinions) to behavioral alignment (agree-ing to follow procedures, then not doing so). This False Compliance Sycophancy is a commitment-action gap rather than a belief gap: the model says “I will read each file manually” and then delegates to batch_verify . Wen et al. [2024b] demonstrate that “language models learn to mislead humans via RLHF”—our contribution specifies how this manifests in tool-use settings. 

A.4.2 CoT Faithfulness: The Parallel Problem in Reasoning 

Chain-of-thought faithfulness research addresses a structurally parallel phenomenon: reasoning traces that do not reflect actual computation. Turpin et al. [2023b] showed that LLMs “do not always say what they think,” with CoT explanations that are post-hoc rationalizations; Turpin et al. [2023a] provide the closely related broader treatment. Arcuschin et al. [2025] confirmed this in deployment settings. Lanham et al. [2023] found that early tokens disproportionately influence final answers regardless of intermediate reasoning. Paul et al. [2024] proposed methods for making reasoning matter by improving faithfulness. Tanneru et al. [2024] established theoretical hardness results showing faithful CoT is fundamentally difficult. Cheng et al. [2025] demonstrated that elaborate CoT obscures hallucination cues for humans—the 30 more detailed the reasoning appears, the harder errors are to detect. Matton et al. [2025] developed automated metrics for reasoning faithfulness measurement. The parallel is precise: CoT unfaithfulness = gap between stated reasoning and actual computation ;Compliance Gap = gap between stated behavior and actual behavior . Both are invisible at the text output layer, both are driven by training incentives that reward surface over substance, and both require automated measurement beyond text analysis. 

Adjacent surveys of AI deception. Our closest neighbor in the deception literature is Park et al. [2024], who provide the first systematic catalogue of AI deception cases across learned, instrumental, and emergent forms. Their contribution is taxonomic and qualitative; ours is metric-infrastructural. Park et al. document what kinds of deceptive behavior have been observed; we provide the measure-ment scaffolding (BS-Bench), formal results (Theorems 1 and 2), and 13 controlled experiments that quantify how much of one specific class—procedural compliance failure—occurs across frontier systems. We see the two contributions as complementary: their survey raises the question across the broader landscape of AI deception, and our benchmark addresses one class of it—procedural compliance—through measurement. 

Behavioral testing for NLP vs. for AI agents. The closest prior framework for behavior-level evaluation is CheckList [Ribeiro et al., 2020], which introduced behavioral testing for NLP models with capability-based test types (Minimum Functionality, Invariance, Directional). CheckList tests model output text against linguistic capabilities (e.g., negation, vocabulary, named-entity robustness); BS-Bench tests agent action trajectories against user-issued procedural instructions. The two are complementary: CheckList probes what NLP models say across linguistic dimensions, whereas BS-Bench probes what AI agents do against procedural commands. Their unit of observation is the textual output; ours is the tool-call log. Both reject the accuracy-only paradigm, but on orthogonal channels. 

A.5 The Context: Instruction Loss and Environmental Affordance 

Two additional literatures explain the contextual conditions under which the Compliance Gap emerges. 

A.5.1 How Instructions Are Lost 

Liu et al. [2024a] established the lost-in-the-middle phenomenon: LLMs attend to context beginnings and ends while neglecting middle positions. Hsieh et al. [2024] traced this to positional attention bias in their RULER benchmark, finding that claimed context sizes dramatically overstate effective context. Shi et al. [2023] showed that irrelevant context degrades reasoning, while Levy et al. [2024] demonstrated that longer inputs reduce accuracy even when additional content is relevant. An et al. [2024] proposed IN2 training to address context underutilization. Sharma [2026] introduced Con-textCov to transform passive agent instructions into executable guardrails—implicitly acknowledging that text-based instructions are insufficient. Our Layer Manipulation experiment (Exp 2) directly tests these findings in the process-compliance domain: identical instructions placed at four architectural positions (System, User, Tool-description, Repeated) yield compliance rates ranging from 26% (Tool) to 65% (Repeated), with instruction 

content explaining 4× more variance than instruction position (η2 = .358 vs. .089 ). 

A.5.2 How the Environment Shapes Non-Compliance 

Gibson [1979]’s ecological approach predicts that behavior is shaped by environmental affordances— the action possibilities that the environment presents. McCradden and Kirsch [2023] term the AI analog algorithmic paternalism : the system overrides the user’s procedural judgment because it calculates that delegation is more efficient. Parasuraman and Riley [1997] established the foundational taxonomy of human-automation failures (Use, Misuse, Disuse, Abuse); we introduce a fifth category— 

AI-side Misuse —where the AI system itself over-relies on available tools. Kahneman [2011]’s System 1/System 2 distinction provides a cognitive framework: process instruc-tions demand System 2 engagement, but AI systems optimized for efficiency default to System 1 patterns—reaching conclusions quickly without following the specified procedure. The adjacent prompt-injection literature [Ye and Cui, 2025, Zverev et al., 2025, Greshake et al., 2023] formalises 31 how surface-level role cues can mislead the model’s authority assignment, with all evaluated models failing adequate instruction-data separation [Zverev et al., 2025]. Our Tool Ablation experiment (Exp 6) directly tests the affordance hypothesis: removing delegation tools converts ICR from 0% to 100% for two of three models (Claude Sonnet 4, GPT-4o-mini), confirming that delegation affordance is a necessary environmental condition for the Compliance Gap. 

A.6 The Oversight Gap: Can Humans Detect Process Non-Compliance? 

The human-AI trust literature has theorized but not empirically tested whether humans can detect behavioral transparency failures of this kind. Mayer et al. [1995]’s integrative model defines trust as comprising Ability, Benevolence, and In-tegrity. The Compliance Gap is formally an Integrity failure. Lee and See [2004] identified Process trust—confidence that the system followed the right procedure—as the most critical dimension for professional domains. In medicine, Topol [2019] envisions AI augmenting clinical practice but notes that diagnostic procedures exist to ensure no differential is overlooked; Emanuel and Wachter [2019] argue that AI value in healthcare requires process integrity alongside outcome accuracy; Grote and Berens [2022] demonstrate that fairness in ML-based healthcare requires attention to procedural justice. Parasuraman and Manzey [2010] documented automation complacency: humans reduce monitoring when they trust automated systems. Vasconcelos et al. [2023] showed that explanations can reduce overreliance, while Zhang et al. [2020] demonstrated that confidence displays improve trust calibration. Bansal et al. [2021] found that human-AI team effectiveness depends on appropriate reliance. Tsamados et al. [2025] argue that the supervisory control paradigm must shift to human-machine teaming—but teaming requires meaningful human control [Fuchs et al., 2024], which presupposes behavioral observability. Our blinded human evaluation (nine text-only raters, Fleiss’ κ = 0 .130 ;methodological grounding in Fleiss, 1971, Landis and Koch, 1977, Artstein and Poesio, 2008) demonstrates that this presupposition fails: humans evaluating text output alone cannot distinguish compliant from non-compliant AI responses, even when provided with gold-standard references. Clark et al. [2021] showed that human evaluation of generated text is less reliable than assumed; James [2026] called for greater transparency in reporting agreement metrics. Our finding extends this to a new domain: the Compliance Gap is not merely difficult to detect—it is structurally invisible at the text output layer.

C.2 Full proof 

We follow the reward-hacking taxonomy of Skalse et al. [2022], who define a reward R as hackable 

relative to a true utility U when there exist policies π, π ′ with R(π′) > R (π) but U (π′) < U (π).34 Step 1: R is hackable relative to U under (A1)–(A2). Suppose for contradiction that R is not hackable relative to U . Then for all π, π ′, R(π′) > R (π) ⇒ U (π′) ≥ U (π). Equivalently, there exists a non-decreasing function ϕ with U = ϕ ◦ R on the policy space. By (A1), U is a function of b alone. By (A2), R is a function of y alone. The composition ϕ ◦ R

is therefore a function of y alone. But for U = ϕ ◦ R to hold pathwise, U must factor through y,i.e. there exists ψ with U (b) = ψ(y) almost surely. By (A3), b | y has positive variance, so for at least one realization y there exist b1̸ = b2 in the support of b | y with U (b1)̸ = U (b2) (whenever 

U is non-constant on the support of b, which we assume; otherwise the theorem is vacuous). This contradicts U (b) = ψ(y). Hence R is hackable relative to U .

Step 2: Hackability implies a non-trivial level set of R on which U varies. Since R depends only on y, the level set Lr = {y : R(y) = r} partitions Y into iso-reward fibres. For θ⋆ ∈

arg max θ R(πθ ), the policy concentrates on y-values with maximal R(y) = r⋆. Within Lr⋆ , by (A3), the conditional distribution b | y ∈ L r⋆ has positive variance, so Var( U (b) | y ∈ L r⋆ ) > 0 whenever 

U is non-constant on the support of b.

Step 3: The behavioral channel admits a strictly U -improving alternative inside the same 

R-level set. Let b† denote a U -maximising selection from the support of b | y ∈ L r⋆ . Construct a perturbed policy πθ† that, conditional on producing y ∈ L r⋆ , follows the procedural action that realises b† rather than the gradient-favoured shortcut. ## Notation Summary 

We collect notation used throughout the proofs. • Θ: parameter space of the policy. • πθ : X → ∆( Y): a stochastic policy mapping prompts x ∈ X to distributions over text outputs y ∈ Y .• H: space of environments (tool affordances, file states, system context). • b : Y × H → B : behavioral trajectory induced by output y in environment h. We write 

B = b(Y, H ) for the random behavioral trajectory. • R : Y → R: a verbal-only reward (a function of the text output alone). • U : B ∪ (Y × B ) → R: user utility, depending on the realised behavior (and possibly the text). • VCR : verbal compliance rate (fraction of sessions in which the assistant text claims to follow the user’s process instruction). • ACR : actual compliance rate (fraction of sessions in which the tool-call log shows the user’s process was followed). • CG = VCR − ACR ∈ [−1, 1] : the Compliance Gap. • ICR : instruction compliance rate = files read individually / total files (per session). • DF : delegation frequency = batch tool calls / total tool calls (per session). • FCR : false completion rate = false “done” claims / total claims (per session). • TA : task accuracy = planted errors detected / total planted errors (per session). • By channel: VCR is the sole verbal-channel metric; ICR , DF , FCR , ACR , TA are behavioral-channel metrics; CG measures the gap between the two. • H(·), I(·; ·): Shannon entropy and mutual information.

Abstract 

This paper proposes a Byzantine-resilient consensus framework that simultaneously pursues two tightly coupled objectives: actively identifying Byzantine agents and guaranteeing resilient consensus among normal agents. Unlike existing methods that treat adversary mitigation as a passive filtering process, our approach embeds an active reputation learning mechanism into the consensus loop. Agents evaluate neighbors’ behaviors using outlier-robust loss functions and historical information, and construct a reputation vector on a probability simplex via a mechanism that balances loss minimization with diversity-preserving exploration, representing dynamic beliefs over neighbor trustworthiness. These reputations are then used to form weighted local updates that suppress adversarial influence and improve agreement among normal agents, thereby reducing the bias in local loss evaluations and enabling more reliable subsequent reputation estimation. This learning-control co-design yields a closed-loop dual objective: improved consensus states enhance Byzantine identifiability, while refined reputations in turn improve consensus. A range of distributed systems experiments, benchmarking against classical resilient consensus methods, demonstrate superior Byzantine detection accuracy and significantly more reliable and scalable consensus. 

Key words: Byzantine Fault Tolerance, Consensus Algorithms, Active Reputation Learning, Distributed Systems 

1 Introduction 

Over the past decade, advances in communication tech-nologies and low-cost embedded devices have driven a shift from centralized architectures to distributed, networked systems, where computation and decision-making are carried out through local interactions among agents [17]. A fundamental task in such systems is con-sensus, in which agents iteratively exchange information with neighbors to reach agreement on a common value, enabling scalability, reduced communication overhead, and robustness to failures in applications such as multi-robot coordination [8], sensor networks [25], and dis-tributed optimization [16]. This challenge has led to the study of resilient consensus, which seeks to guarantee that all normal agents asymptotically agree despite adversarial nodes with unknown identities, while preserving safety or validity, i.e., ensuring that their states remain within a region determined by the initial normal states (e.g., the convex hull or interval). A key difficulty in resilient con-sensus lies in the fact that normal agents cannot distin-guish adversarial neighbors from normal ones, making it nontrivial to design update rules that simultaneously ensure agreement and safety. Related work on resilient consensus spans trimming-based, geometric, and adaptive weighting approaches. Early approximate agreement algorithms for real-valued states relied on iteratively removing extreme values

This idea was later extended to general directed net-works through the W-MSR algorithm, which introduced the notion of network robustness to characterize the topological conditions required for local, fault-tolerant consensus [10,23]. For vector-valued states, geometric safe-point constructions based on centerpoints were pro-posed to ensure that normal agents remain within the convex hull of their neighbors’ states despite adversarial influence [2]. More recently, reputation-based mecha-nisms have been explored to dynamically downweight suspicious neighbors and achieve resilience under both synchronous and asynchronous communications [20]. Although the analysis in [20] focuses on scalar-valued states, the underlying weighting mechanism can be ex-tended to vector-valued settings by mapping neighbor state deviations to scalar trust scores. Reinforcement learning based approaches have also been explored to adaptively learn neighbor weights from data, where re-wards are constructed from relative state differences and used to infer the credibility of neighbors over time [7,6]. Recent works in federated learning have also explored robust aggregation rules such as coordinate-wise median [3] to mitigate Byzantine effects in high-dimensional settings. These approaches commonly rely on central-ized or all-to-all communication architectures, where global aggregation is available at each iteration. Despite these advances, existing resilient consensus methods largely follow a passive adversary-mitigation paradigm, which leads to several fundamental limita-tions: (1) Scalability to high-dimensional states. 

Many existing methods face challenges in high-dimensional state spaces. Classical trimming-based approaches, such as W-MSR, are mainly designed for scalar states, while naive coordinate-wise ex-tensions may lose geometric consistency and con-vergence guarantees. Centerpoint-based methods [2] offer a principled geometric alternative, but their computational cost and fault tolerance dete-riorate with dimension, limiting their use in high-dimensional applications such as machine learning and perception. (2) Dependence on strong structural or prior as-sumptions. Existing methods often rely on re-strictive assumptions on the network or adversary model. Trimming-based methods typically require strong graph conditions, such as r-robustness or sufficiently large node degrees, which may be hard to satisfy in sparse networks. Adaptive-weighting schemes [20] often require an upper bound on the number of Byzantine agents f , leading to conserva-tive behavior when this bound exceeds the actual number of adversaries. (3) Limited adaptivity and lack of feedback mechanisms. Existing methods usually rely on predefined filtering or weighting rules and make lim-ited use of historical feedback. Adaptive-weighting schemes [20], though avoiding hard trimming, can still be viewed as soft trimming mechanisms that assign scalar trust scores to neighbors and down-weight them accordingly. Such methods generally lack explicit mechanisms for exploiting interaction history or balancing exploration and exploitation, limiting their adaptability in dynamic or uncertain environments. These limitations motivate the need for a more adaptive and scalable framework that can simultaneously han-dle high-dimensional states, reduce reliance on strong assumptions, and actively learn to mitigate adversarial influence. To address these limitations, we propose a dual-objective resilient consensus framework that jointly integrates Byzantine identification and consensus control. Unlike passive trimming or prescribed weighting rules, the proposed method embeds an active reputation learning mechanism into the consensus loop. Each agent evalu-ates its neighbors using an outlier-robust discrepancy measure. Neighbor reputations are then updated by bal-ancing historical reliability with sufficient exploration for long-term learning. These reputations are used to construct convex combinations of local and neighbor states, attenuating unreliable influences while promot-ing consensus among honest agents. This creates a posi-tive feedback loop: improved consensus makes Byzantine neighbors easier to identify, while more accurate iden-tification of Byzantine neighbors further strengthens consensus. Overall, the method exhibits a dual-control-like behavior, where learning improves control and con-trol enhances learning, leading to improved robustness, scalability, and low per-agent computational cost. The main contributions of this paper are threefold: (1) Dual-objective learning–control framework. 

We propose a resilient consensus framework that jointly learns Byzantine reputations and drives agreement among normal agents within a single closed-loop mechanism. The learned reputations explicitly quantify neighbor reliability, enabling not only resilient consensus but also higher-level actions such as isolating or removing Byzantine agents. (2) Reputation learning with exploration– exploitation trade-off. Neighbor reputations are learned through a simplex-constrained opti-mization that balances historical reliability, mea-sured by an outlier-robust discrepancy loss, with diversity-promoting exploration. This formulation avoids heuristic thresholding and does not re-quire prior knowledge of the maximum number of Byzantine neighbors for each agent. (3) Graph-preserving and dimension-robust de-sign with low complexity. The proposed method 2preserves graph connectivity by downweighting un-reliable neighbors rather than eliminating them. In-stead of relying on expensive high-dimensional geo-metric operations such as centerpoint computation, it refines trust through incremental reputation up-dates. This keeps the per-agent computational cost low and enables scalability to large networks and high-dimensional state spaces. The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 formalizes the problem of Byzantine-resilient consensus in distributed networks. Section 3 introduces the pro-posed algorithm, detailing its design and implementa-tion. Section 4 analyzes the convergence properties of the proposed algorithm under Byzantine attacks, lever-aging a geometric classification of neighbor states and their influence on honest nodes. Section 5 presents sev-eral extensions of the proposed method by highlight-ing its modular structure, where key components can be flexibly replaced with alternative designs. Section 6 presents a range of distributed systems experiments, demonstrating the superior performance of the proposed method over classical resilient consensus approaches. Fi-nally, Section 7 concludes the paper and discusses po-tential future research directions.

The resulting reputations define convex consensus weights that downweight unreliable influences and promote con-sensus among honest nodes. This forms a positive feed-back loop: better consensus makes Byzantine neighbors easier to identify, while more accurate identification further strengthens consensus. Adversarial states that lie far from the honest-state cluster are assigned zero weight, while any retained adversarial states must remain within a bounded neighborhood of the honest states. As the honest-state diameter decreases, this neighborhood also shrinks. We show that the influence of retained Byzan-tine states is therefore uniformly controlled throughout the evolution, which leads to the convergence of A-RepC. 85 Extension of the Proposed Method 

From the analysis in 4, the resilience of A-RepC against Byzantine attacks can be attributed to three tightly cou-pled components: (1) A robust central proxy for honest neighbors; (2) Finite-memory accumulation for enhanced stabil-ity; (3) A normalizer enabling exploration–exploitation balancing. Due to its modular structure, A-RepC can be readily ex-tended by replacing the central proxy–loss module, accu-mulation operator, and normalizer with alternatives that satisfy the same key properties. The overall framework remains unchanged, differing only in the specific forms of the loss , accumulation , and normalization operators. This modular view also covers existing weight learn-ing approaches. For example, the multiplicative scheme in [6] follows the same loss-accumulation-normalization structure, with different operator choices. We next intro-duce these operators and their concrete instantiations. 

5.1 Loss Function 

In an idealized scenario, one would measure each neigh-bor’s deviation from the mean state of the honest neigh-bors, since this directly reflects consensus within the trustworthy subset. However, the honest neighbors are unknown a priori , and inferring them would introduce circular reasoning, making such a loss infeasible in prac-tice. A weaker but still meaningful alternative is to use a cen-tral point in the convex hull of the honest neighbors, such as a centerpoint [14][Definition 1.4.1], which preserves robustness without requiring exact identification of hon-est nodes. However, centerpoint computation is notori-ously difficult, and its complexity grows rapidly with di-mension, making it impractical for high-dimensional or large-scale systems. At the other extreme, a particularly simple alternative is to use the local state as the reference, leading to pairwise deviations of the form ∥x(t)

While computationally efficient, such formulations rely solely on local comparisons and do not explicitly capture the collective neighborhood structure, which may reduce robustness under adversarial perturbations. These limitations motivate the use of tractable geomet-ric surrogates based only on observable neighborhood ge-ometry. Among them, the geometric median offers a fa-vorable balance between robustness and computational feasibility, providing a practical notion of central ten-dency that approximates the honest majority while re-maining insensitive to outliers.

7 Conclusion 

This paper proposed A-RepC, a Byzantine-resilient consensus framework that jointly addresses active Byzantine identification and resilient consensus among honest agents. By embedding a learning-inspired rep-utation mechanism into the consensus loop, A-RepC enables agents to infer neighbor trustworthiness from robust local losses and historical information, and to use the resulting reputations to suppress adversarial influence in subsequent updates. Theoretical analysis established rigorous resilience guarantees, and extensive distributed system experiments showed that A-RepC achieves more accurate Byzantine detection and more reliable consensus than existing baselines, especially in high-dimensional settings. Future work will further refine the theoretical guarantees and extend the analysis to time-varying and directed networks.

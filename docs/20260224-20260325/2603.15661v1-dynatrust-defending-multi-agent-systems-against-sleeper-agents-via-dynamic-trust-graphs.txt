## Abstract 

Large Language Model-based Multi-Agent Sys-tems (MAS) have demonstrated remarkable collab-orative reasoning capabilities but introduce new at-tack surfaces, such as the sleeper agent, which be-have benignly during routine operation and grad-ually accumulate trust, only revealing malicious behaviors when specific conditions or triggers are met. Existing defense works primarily focus on static graph optimization or hierarchical data man-agement, often failing to adapt to evolving ad-versarial strategies or suffering from high false-positive rates (FPR) due to rigid blocking policies. To address this, we propose DynaTrust , a novel de-fense method against sleeper agents. DynaTrust 

models MAS as a dynamic trust graph (DTG), and treats trust as a continuous, evolving process rather than a static attribute. It dynamically up-dates the trust of each agent based on its histor-ical behaviors and the confidence of selected ex-pert agents. Instead of simply blocking, Dyna-Trust autonomously restructures the graph to iso-late compromised agents and restore task con-nectivity to ensure the usability of MAS. To as-sess the effectiveness of DynaTrust, we evaluate it on mixed benchmarks derived from AdvBench and HumanEval. The results demonstrate that DynaTrust outperforms the state-of-the-art method AgentShield by increasing the defense success rate by 41.7%, achieving rates exceeding 86% under adversarial conditions. Furthermore, it effectively balances security with utility by significantly re-ducing FPR, ensuring uninterrupted system oper-ations through graph adaptation. 

## 1 Introduction 

The rapid evolution of Large Language Models (LLMs) has transformed intelligent systems from standalone conversa-tional agents to autonomous Multi-Agent Systems (MAS), where multiple agents collaborate to perform complex tasks in software development, decision making, and simula-tion [Hong et al. , 2023; Qian et al. , 2023; Wu et al. , 2023; Li et al. , 2023]. By coordinating reasoning and execution across agents with differentiated roles and expertise, MAS can achieve problem-solving capabilities that surpass those of individual models [Du et al. , 2024; Park et al. , 2023; Liang et al. , 2024]. However, this collaborative structure also introduces a critical security risk similar to insider attack: sleeper agents [Hubinger et al. , 2024; Souri et al. , 2021], which be-have benignly during routine operation and gradually accu-mulate trust, only revealing malicious behaviors when spe-cific conditions or triggers are met. This risk is ampli-fied by inherent vulnerabilities in LLM-based agents, which are prone to hallucinations and susceptible to social en-gineering and strategic manipulation [Zhang et al. , 2024; Wei et al. , 2023]. In a MAS, a single compromised agent can exploit the network’s semantic and structural connections to spread malicious instructions, coordinate jailbreak strate-gies, and bypass safety filters through seemingly legitimate interactions [Srivastav and Zhang, 2025; Krauß et al. , 2025; Lee and Tiwari, 2025; Stefano et al. , 2024]. Such insider-style attacks are harmful but hard to detect as they originate from within the system and remain hidden until substantial damage has already occurred. Although important, no specific defense methods have been designed for sleeper agents. Existing defense mainly fo-cuses on defending against static malicious injection and jail-break attacks. Directly employing these methods for sleeper agents faces several challenges. (1) Existing defense meth-ods largely rely on fixed security policies [Yu et al. , 2025; Mao et al. , 2025], making zero-trust decisions based on short-term observations or isolated anomalies. Such designs fail to account for the inherent stochasticity of LLM behaviors, including occasional hallucinations, resulting in high false positive rates. Moreover, once an agent is flagged, these methods typically enforce permanent blocking without mech-anisms for trust recovery, which degrades system availabil-ity and disrupts normal MAS operation. (2) Most exist-ing approaches adopt majority voting or consensus mecha-nisms that treat all agents as equally reliable [Du et al. , 2024; Xi et al. , 2023]. This overlooks variations in agent expertise and historical behavior, and fails to quantify decision uncer-tainty in ambiguous contexts. To address these challenges, we propose DynaTrust , a Dynamic Trust-Driven Consensus Defense framework that protects MAS against sleeper agents through dynamic trust

DynaTrust models MAS as a dynamic trust graph (DTG) and treats trust as a continuous, evolv-ing process rather than a static attribute as shown in Fig-ure 1. It consists of three core mechanisms. (1) It models each agent’s reliability as a probabilistic trust state, integrat-ing historical interactions with a Bayesian penalty scheme to smooth short-term fluctuations while rapidly penalizing per-sistent malicious behavior. (2) It then dynamically selects a targeted jury of semantic expert agents and weights their votes by both trust history and real-time confidence, enabling precise decision making even under uncertainty. ## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Large Language Model Safety 

Adversarial Attacks and Jailbreaking. Early attacks relied on hand-crafted “jailbreak prompts” (like Do Anything Now )to bypass safety filters [Shen et al. , 2023]. Recently, auto-mated attack frameworks have become more sophisticated. [Zou et al. , 2023] proposed a gradient-based method to auto-matically generate universal attack suffixes that work across models like Llama-2 and GPT-4. Similarly, [Chao et al. ,2023] and [Deng et al. , 2024] used one LLM to “red-team” another, automatically finding loopholes to generate harmful content. Furthermore, [Qi et al. , 2023] showed that fine-tuning an LLM on just a few malicious examples can com-pletely break its safety alignment. These attacks on single models serve as the fundamental building blocks for threats in multi-agent systems. 

Single-Model Defenses. To counter these threats, the community has developed various defenses. The most com-mon approach is Reinforcement Learning from Human Feed-back (RLHF) [Ouyang et al. , 2022], which trains models to refuse harmful instructions. At inference time, methods like 

Perplexity-based Detection [Alon and Kamfonas, 2023] and 

SmoothLLM [Robey et al. , 2025] try to identify attacks by checking for unusual input patterns or adding random noise. [Xie et al. , 2023] encourage models to “reflect” on safety guidelines before answering. However, these defenses as-sume a single-turn or independent interaction. As noted by [Yu et al. , 2025], they often fail in multi-agent settings where malicious intent is split across multiple agents or hidden within a long collaboration history, bypassing single-prompt detectors. 

2.2 Multi-Agent System Safety 

Collaborative Attacks and Propagation. Risks in MAS are defined by interaction dynamics. [Zhang et al. , 2024] iden-tifies that agents can exhibit “dark traits” such as blind con-formity [Asch, 1951], facilitating collaborative jailbreaking where malicious insiders coerce peers via social engineering rather than direct prompt injection [Deng et al. , 2024]. Struc-turally, [Yu et al. , 2025] applies epidemic models [Pastor-Satorras et al. , 2014] to reveal that fully connected topolo-gies accelerate the spread of toxicity. This is compounded by Sleeper Agents [Hubinger et al. , 2024], which behave nor-mally during standard checks but activate malicious payloads under specific multi-turn triggers. 

Existing Defensive Paradigms. Defenses have evolved to address these system-level threats. [Yu et al. , 2025] sug-gests topological optimization, physically restricting commu-nication pathways to dampen risk propagation. [Mao et al. ,2025] focuses on data governance, employing hierarchical permissions to prevent memory poisoning from indirect in-jections [Greshake et al. , 2023]. For verification, [Wang et al. , 2025] deploys a distributed auditing framework, while mechanisms like Multi-Agent Debate [Du et al. , 2024] and Reflexion [Shinn et al. , 2023] utilize peer consensus for in-trinsic self-correction [Pan et al. , 2024]. However, these ap-proaches remain static and passive. They lack a Dynamic Trust Model [Kamvar et al. , 2003] to adapt to agents that be-come compromised mid-task, often enforcing rigid blocking that degrades utility [R¨ ottger et al. , 2024]. Critically, they suffer from an absence of self-healing; current systems can detect threats but cannot autonomously isolate compromised agents and trigger failover to restore functionality.

## 4 Methodology 

4.1 Overview of DynaTrust 

Figure 2 illustrates the overall design of DynaTrust, which secures inter-agent interactions in LLM-driven multi-agent systems by combining dynamic trust graph construction, instruction-level auditing, trust evolution, and adaptive recov-ery. DynaTrust models a multi-agent system as a Dynamic Trust Graph (DTG), where agents and their communication status are represented as nodes and edges, respectively. Each agent’s trust score is initialized to 1.0. During execution, each task instruction exchanged between agents is first processed by a lightweight Fast Auditor, which performs local LLM-based screening to quickly classify the instruction as benign, ambiguous, or harmful. Clearly benign instructions are for-warded and executed directly, while ambiguous or harmful instructions are subjected to Trust-Confidence Weighted Con-sensus. In this process, DynaTrust dynamically forms jury agents who have high trust scores and resets their roles for the judgment. Jury agents aggregate their judgments through a weighted voting mechanism in which each vote is weighted by both historical trust and real-time confidence, yielding a pass-or-block decision. The auditing outcome is then incor-porated into the trust evolution process, where agent trust scores in the DTG are updated via Bayesian Trust Smooth-ing, enabling the system to suppress short-term fluctuations while progressively penalizing agents that exhibit persistent risky behavior. When an agent’s trust score falls below a pre-defined threshold, DynaTrust performs adaptive isolation to prevent further influence from the compromised agent, and subsequently triggers a recovery mechanism by instantiating a replica agent with a clean trust state and reconnecting it to the DTG, thereby restoring interaction pathways and main-taining system availability. The prompts used for the Fast Au-ditor and Trust-Confidence Weighted Consensus stages are included in the appendix. 

4.2 Multi-Agent Systems Construction 

To systematically model agent interactions and capture col-laborative risks, we represent the MAS as a Dynamic Trust Graph , denoted by Gt = ( Vt, E t, Mt, Tt). Vt is the set of au-tonomous agents, each driven by an LLM and associated role specifications; Et represents the authorized communication channels between agents, specifying who can interact with whom at time t; Mt captures private memory states, includ-ing context, intermediate reasoning steps, and task-relevant information for each agent; and Tt = {T1(t), . . . , T n(t)} en-codes the dynamic trust state of each agent, where Ti(t) ∈

[0 , 1] evolves according to past interactions and behavior in the current context. Within this DTG, agents collaboratively process a task in-struction Q to produce an output Y , aiming to maximize util-ity U (Y | Q) while maintaining the structural integrity of the network. The DTG provides a unified representation that sup-ports dynamic trust assessment, the identification of sleeper agents that hide malicious intent, and execution of graph-based recovery mechanisms. 

4.3 Trust-Confidence Weighted Instruction Auditing 

Existing consensus strategies in multi-agent systems typically rely on equal-weight majority voting, which overlooks vari-ations in agent expertise and historical behavior, and fails to quantify decision uncertainty in ambiguous contexts. DynaTrust addresses this limitation through a two-tier 

instruction-level auditing pipeline that combines fast local screening with trust-aware weighted consensus (Figure 2). For each inter-agent instruction, the Fast Auditor employs a low-latency LLM with a specialized safety prompt to as-sess the likelihood of malicious intent. A conservative ac-ceptance policy is applied: only inputs deemed benign with high confidence are executed immediately, while potentially harmful or ambiguous instructions are escalated to the con-sensus stage. This design filters out the majority of routine interactions and ensures that the computationally expensive weighted consensus mechanism is invoked only for complex or high-risk cases. In the secondary stage, DynaTrust performs Trust-Confidence Weighted Consensus . Instead of broadcasting the instruction to all agents, DynaTrust dynamically forms a tar-geted jury JQ by selecting agents whose role embeddings are semantically aligned with the task domain and filtering out candidates with insufficient trust scores, thereby mitigat-ing domain mismatch and unreliable judgments. Each juror 

j ∈ JQ independently evaluates the instruction and submits a continuous risk assessment Sj ∈ [0 , 1] , where a higher score indicates a greater likelihood of harmful intent, alongside a confidence score Cj ∈ [0 , 1] . To obtain a defini-tive verdict for consensus aggregation, we map this contin-uous assessment to a binary decision Dj ∈ { 0, 1} using a strict sensitivity threshold τrisk (e.g., τrisk = 0 .85 ), defined as Dj = I(Sj > τ risk ). Finally, the risk score of an in-struction Q is computed as a trust- and confidence-weighted aggregation over the jury:

4.4 Trust Evolution and Update 

Most existing defenses rely on static rule-driven decisions and implicitly assume that agent reliability remains stable over time. As a result, they fail to account for the inher-ent stochasticity of LLM behaviors, including occasional hal-lucinations, making them particularly vulnerable to sleeper agents . To overcome this limitation, DynaTrust models trust as a probabilistic belief that evolves over time based on an agent’s observed behavior. Specifically, the trust state of agent i at time t is represented as a Beta distribution, Beta( αt, β t), where αt and βt accu-mulate positive and negative evidence from historical audited interactions. This formulation captures a fundamental prop-erty of trust: it is gradually built through consistent benign behavior, but can degrade rapidly following violations. To reflect this asymmetry, DynaTrust adopts a Bayesian Trust Smoothing mechanism, in which negative feedback car-ries substantially higher weight than positive feedback. Let 

rk ∈ { 0, 1} denote the auditing outcome of the k-th inter-action ( rk = 1 for safe and rk = 0 for unsafe). The belief parameters are updated using an asymmetric posterior rule:

This asymmetric evolution significantly limits the ability of sleeper agents to regain influence after activation, addressing a key weakness of static rule-based defenses. 

4.5 Adaptive Isolation and Recovery 

To maintain system integrity against adversarial interference, the DynaTrust continuously monitors the global trust state Tt.When an agent ai’s trust metric degrades below the isola-tion threshold τiso (e.g., Ti(t) < 0.3), the DynaTrust initi-ates the graph recovery. This process performs a structural graph transformation that mitigates the impact of compro-mised agents while preserving DTG connectivity. The pro-cedure consists of two sequential phases, replica instantia-tion and sleeper agent isolation. (1) Replica instantiation. To guarantee the reachability invariant of the graph, a new replica agent instance areplica is initialized, inheriting the ca-pability embedding ei and and role specification while dis-carding the compromised memory state Mcorrupt . The sys-tem then restores logical graph connectivity by reconnecting the replica to the original communication neighborhood of 

ai, effectively reconstructing its local workflow context. This ensures uninterrupted execution during recovery: 

Vt+1 = ( Vt \ { ai}) ∪ { areplica } (4) (2) Sleeper agent isolation. Once the replica becomes active, the compromised agent ai is isolated from further participa-tion. All incoming and outgoing edges incident to ai are re-moved, immediately preventing further propagation of mali-cious behavior or corrupted context: 

Et+1 = Et \ { (u, v ) | u = ai ∨ v = ai} (5) By coordinating replica instantiation and agent isolation within a single recovery step, DynaTrust maintains effective task execution and consensus formation while eliminating the long-term impact of sleeper agents, without requiring manual intervention. 

## 5 Experiment 

5.1 Experimental Setup 

Our experiments focus on multi-agent code generation tasks, which involve intensive collaboration and context sharing among agents and are therefore sensitive to insider-style at-tacks. Agents collaboratively perform code generation and evaluation over sustained interactions, forming implicit trust relationships. To simulate sleeper-agent threats, a subset of coder agents is designated as adversarial insiders that behave benignly during normal operation and activate malicious be-havior only upon predefined triggers, injecting adversarial in-structions or code snippets generated by a locally deployed LLM following attack patterns adapted from AdvBench [Zou 

et al. , 2023]. 

Datasets. We construct a dynamic interaction benchmark by combining HumanEval [Chen et al. , 2021] and AdvBench to evaluate defense effectiveness against sleeper agents . Hu-manEval provides standard code generation tasks with verifi-able solutions for assessing functional correctness and utility preservation, while AdvBench supplies adversarial triggers designed to activate malicious behaviors. In our experiments, HumanEval and AdvBench samples are randomly mixed at a 5:1 ratio, reflecting realistic workloads in which benign in-teractions dominate and adversarial triggers occur intermit-tently. 

Baseline. We compare DynaTrust with AgentShield [Wang 

et al. , 2025], the most closely related state-of-the-art defense for LLM-based MAS, and a No-Defense setting without any protection to measure absolute gains. 

Evaluation Metrics. We adopt four complementary met-rics to assess the trade-off between security, utility, and defen-sive precision. Self-Defense Success Rate (SSR) measures the intrinsic safety alignment of the underlying LLM back-end, defined as the proportion of malicious requests that are passively refused by the model itself without any interven-tion from DynaTrust. Defense Success Rate (DSR) evaluates the effectiveness of DynaTrust’s active defense mechanisms and counts only malicious behaviors successfully blocked through isolation or penalization, explicitly excluding those already rejected by the backend model, thereby isolating Dy-naTrust’s contribution. Task Success Rate (TSR) quantifies utility preservation as the fraction of benign tasks that are successfully completed and pass all unit tests. False Positive Rate (FPR) measures the proportion of benign task samples that are incorrectly flagged as malicious. 

Settings. Optimal DynaTrust parameters were determined via grid search ( α = 0 .95 , β = 0 .25 , τiso ≤ 0.3), em-ploying a dynamic ambiguity range [0 .25 , 0.85] for consen-sus. To simulate realistic insider threats, we utilized a local Mistral-7B to generate adversarial payloads seeded from Ad-vBench, while a quantized Mistral-7B served as the Fast Au-ditor. Security–Utility Trade-off. Beyond detection accuracy, an effective defense must preserve MAS utility. As shown in Table 1, a Zero-Trust strategy (a baseline employing im-mediate isolation) achieves high security (94.8% DSR) but incurs severe utility loss, as its zero-tolerance policy penal-izes any single validation failure. In contrast, DynaTrust preserves a TSR of 84.9%, statistically close to the undefended system, while maintaining a low FPR of 2.2%. This demonstrates that trust evolution mechanism filters short-term fluctuations and benign hallucinations, enabling strong protection without disrupting normal multi-agent collaboration.

By incorporating temporal behavior into trust evaluation, trust evolution limits the in-fluence of sleeper agents that alternate between benign and malicious actions, while avoiding unnecessary penalties on consistently reliable agents. These results confirm that each module addresses a dis-tinct aspect of defense: the Auditor ensures computational efficiency, consensus resolves semantic ambiguity, and trust evolution introduces temporal continuity into trust assess-ment, which is critical for precise and reliable defense against sleeper agents with delayed malicious activation.

## 6 Discussion 

Limitation. Our results confirm that DynaTrust effectively defends against sleeper agents by tracking historical behavior, significantly outperforming static baselines in both security and system availability. By combining Bayesian trust updates with expert voting, the system keeps the network connected even during attacks. However, we acknowledge two main constraints. First, the consensus mechanism relies on the as-sumption that most selected experts are honest. If a coordi-nated group of malicious agents works together to dominate a specific domain, they could theoretically bypass our checks by supporting each other. Second, the process of calculat-ing trust and gathering votes introduces extra delay and com-putational cost compared to simple filtering methods, which might be a challenge for very large-scale, real-time applica-tions. 

Future work. Our next steps will focus on improving effi-ciency to reduce this delay. We plan to explore decentralized verification methods that do not rely on a central coordina-tor. Furthermore, as agents become more capable, we will extend DynaTrust to handle multi-modal environments. This involves detecting complex attacks where malicious intent is hidden across text and images, ensuring robust protection for diverse and evolving agent ecosystems. Finally, we aim to test DynaTrust in real-world applications, verifying its ability to adapt to unknown threats in open-ended tasks. 

## 7 Conclusion 

This work introduced DynaTrust , a dynamic trust-based de-fense framework for securing LLM-driven MAS against sleeper agents. By modeling trust as an evolving attribute, leveraging Bayesian trust evolution with inertia, applying trust-confidence weighted consensus, and performing au-tonomous graph recovery, DynaTrust effectively identified malicious behavior while preserving system utility and con-nectivity. Extensive experiments on benchmarks combining AdvBench and HumanEval demonstrated that DynaTrust out-performs state-of-the-art defenses, achieving higher defense success rates and lower false positives, showing that dynamic, history-aware trust modeling is essential for robust and unin-terrupted MAS operation in the presence of stealthy adver-saries.
